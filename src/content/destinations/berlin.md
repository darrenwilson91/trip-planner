## Things to Do

Spend a morning at the East Side Gallery — it's the longest surviving stretch of the Berlin Wall, covered in murals, running along the River Spree. It's free and open-air, so there's no queue. From there, walk across the Oberbaum Bridge (the most handsome bridge in Berlin) into Kreuzberg, which is where the city's character really lives. Oranienstraße has Turkish grocery shops, punk bars, and independent record stores coexisting without apparent contradiction.

The flea markets are brilliant: Mauerpark on Sundays is the biggest and best known (the karaoke in the amphitheatre is a spectacle), but Nowkoelln Flowmarkt along the canal in Neukölln is more curated and less hectic. For something properly different, rent a canoe on the Landwehr Canal — you paddle through the middle of Kreuzberg, under bridges, past people barbecuing in Görlitzer Park, and the perspective on the city is completely different from walking.

## The Vibe

Berlin doesn't try to impress you and that's exactly what makes it impressive. The city has a deliberate lack of polish — buildings still have bullet holes, clubs are in abandoned power stations, restaurants open in former shops with mismatched furniture and no signage. It's a creative city in the genuine sense, not in the marketing sense. Rents are rising but still affordable by London or Paris standards, which means artists and musicians can actually live there.

Kreuzberg is the heart of multicultural Berlin — the Turkish community has been there for decades and the food reflects it. Neukölln next door has shifted from working-class to creative in the last ten years. Prenzlauer Berg is family-friendly and leafy, with weekend brunch culture that would rival any city. Mitte is the tourist and government district — impressive but less characterful. Friedrichshain is where the nightlife concentrates, though Berlin's clubs famously have no closing times and the serious ones don't open until Saturday midnight.

## Things to See

The Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe (the Holocaust Memorial) near the Brandenburg Gate is one of the most powerful monuments you'll ever visit — 2,711 concrete blocks of varying heights that you walk through. The underground information centre beneath it is harrowing and essential. The Topography of Terror, built on the former Gestapo headquarters, documents the Nazi security apparatus and is free.

Museum Island in the Mitte district has five museums clustered together — the Pergamon Museum (temporarily partially closed for renovation — check current status) and the Neues Museum (with the bust of Nefertiti) are the highlights. For contemporary art, the Hamburger Bahnhof in a converted railway station has an outstanding collection. The Reichstag dome is free to visit but you need to register online in advance — the views from the glass dome across the Tiergarten and city skyline are worth the logistics.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Berlin is a comfortable city. Traditional German food rarely uses nuts — schnitzel, bratwurst, kartoffelsalat (potato salad), sauerkraut, pretzels, spätzle are all naturally nut-free. Germany's allergen labelling regulations are thorough, and restaurant menus typically have allergen codes or footnotes. The main areas to check: marzipan appears in some German cakes and Christmas sweets, and Berlin's huge international food scene means you'll encounter cuisines that do use nuts (Turkish, Thai, Vietnamese, Indian). But you'll be clearly informed in most places. Döner kebabs — Berlin's de facto street food — are nut-free. "Ich habe eine Nussallergie" is the German phrase, though English is widely spoken in restaurants across the city.

For vegetarians, Berlin is extraordinary. The city has more vegans per capita than almost anywhere in Europe and the food infrastructure reflects it. Kopps in Mitte does upscale vegan dining. Lucky Leek in Prenzlauer Berg offers a full vegan tasting menu. But you don't need dedicated restaurants — Mustafa's Gemüse Kebap (the legendary vegetable kebab near Mehringdamm) has a queue for a reason, and most of Kreuzberg's Turkish restaurants do excellent vegetable-based mezes, falafel, and gözleme. Markthalle Neun in Kreuzberg hosts a weekly Street Food Thursday with loads of vegetarian options. Even traditional German beer halls are adapting — vegan schnitzel and plant-based currywurst are becoming standard. Supermarkets like Veganz (fully vegan) make self-catering easy. You will eat better as a vegetarian in Berlin than in almost any other European city.
