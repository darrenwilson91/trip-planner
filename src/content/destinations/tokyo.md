## Things to Do

Start in Shinjuku — not because it's pretty (it isn't, particularly) but because the sheer scale of it recalibrates your sense of what a city can be. The station alone handles over three million passengers a day. Walk through the west side's skyscraper district, then cross over to Kabukicho, which has cleaned up a lot in recent years but still has an energy unlike anywhere else. Golden Gai, tucked behind the main drag, is a grid of around two hundred tiny bars, each seating maybe six people. Pick one that looks interesting and just go in.

Shibuya's famous crossing is worth seeing once — stand at the Starbucks on the second floor of the Tsutaya building for the best view. But Shibuya itself is mostly shopping. Better to walk south from there to Daikanyama and Naka-Meguro, which are quieter, full of independent shops, bookstores (Tsutaya's flagship in Daikanyama is gorgeous), and canal-side cafés. In spring, the Meguro River cherry blossom walk from here is one of the best in Tokyo.

For a completely different pace, spend a morning at Meiji Shrine in Harajuku. The forested approach — a wide gravel path through towering camphor trees — feels like stepping out of the city entirely. Afterwards, Takeshita Street is the famous teen-fashion strip, but turn off onto Cat Street for better shopping and fewer crowds. Omotesando boulevard, sometimes called Tokyo's Champs-Élysées, has incredible architecture — every luxury brand hired a different starchitect for their building.

## The Vibe

Tokyo operates on a level of precision and consideration that takes a few days to fully register. Trains are on time to the second. Packaging is immaculate. Nobody eats while walking. The politeness is genuine, not performative, and it comes with a respect for personal space that makes the density of the city feel surprisingly calm. You can sit in a packed ramen shop with twelve strangers and it somehow feels private.

The city has layers — hyper-modern Akihabara with its anime billboards and maid cafés sits a twenty-minute walk from Yanaka, where elderly shopkeepers tend wooden-fronted stores on lanes that survived the war. Shimokitazawa, west of Shibuya, has the vintage shops and live music venues. Koenji has the punk bars. Each neighbourhood has its own distinct character, and the train system makes hopping between them effortless once you've got a Suica card sorted.

Nights out are different here. People tend to eat at one place, drink at another, then move on — the bar-hopping culture in areas like Ebisu, Sangenjaya, or the standing bars (tachinomiya) under the Yurakucho rail tracks is brilliant. Last trains are around midnight, but taxis are expensive and Uber barely exists — either call it a night or commit to staying out until the first trains at five.

## Things to See

Senso-ji in Asakusa is Tokyo's oldest temple and worth an early morning visit before the tourist crowds arrive. The Nakamise shopping street leading up to it sells rice crackers, fans, and tourist tat, but the temple itself is properly atmospheric — especially if you catch the incense smoke drifting through the main hall. Nearby, the Sumida River walk gives you good views of the Tokyo Skytree.

The Tsukiji Outer Market (the inner wholesale market moved to Toyosu in 2018) is still very much operational and one of the best food experiences in the city. Go before 9am. The tamagoyaki stalls, fresh uni, and grilled scallops on sticks are all worth queueing for. Toyosu Market does offer guided tours if you want to see the tuna auction, but book well ahead.

teamLab Borderless (currently at Azabudai Hills after relocating from Odaiba) is genuinely impressive digital art — not just Instagram fodder. Give it at least two hours. In Ueno, the Tokyo National Museum has the best collection of Japanese art and artefacts in the country, and the park around it is another excellent cherry blossom spot.

For something quieter, take the train to Kamakura (about an hour south) to see the Great Buddha and hike between temples on the Daibutsu trail. Or head to Nikko for ornate Tokugawa-era shrines set in mountain forest — a proper day trip that feels like a different country.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

This needs saying plainly: Tokyo is one of the harder destinations in this guide if you have a nut allergy. Japanese cuisine uses sesame seeds and sesame oil in almost everything — dressings, sauces, stir-fries, and as a garnish. Peanuts appear in some sauces and as a topping, particularly in regional dishes. Tree nuts show up in wagashi (traditional sweets) and some modern bakeries.

The real difficulty is communication. Many restaurant staff don't speak conversational English, and the concept of food allergies is understood differently in Japan — there's less of a culture of asking about ingredients, and cross-contamination isn't something most kitchens think about the way UK restaurants do. A printed allergy card in Japanese is not optional — it's essential. Get one that lists your specific allergies in Japanese script. Show it to the server before ordering, and ideally to the chef.

Chain restaurants and department store food halls (depachika) are actually your safest bet — many have allergen information printed on menus. Convenience stores like 7-Eleven, Lawson, and FamilyMart label allergens on packaged food by law (look for the Japanese allergen icons). For vegetarians, the challenge is equally real. Dashi is everywhere. Even plain-looking tofu or vegetable tempura may be fried in sesame oil or served in dashi-based sauce. Your best options are shojin ryori restaurants (temple cuisine, entirely plant-based), Indian restaurants in areas like Nishi-Shinjuku, and the growing number of vegan-friendly spots in Shibuya and Shimokitazawa. Download the HappyCow app before you go — it will save you repeatedly.
