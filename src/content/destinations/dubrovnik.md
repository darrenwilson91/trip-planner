## Things to Do

Walk the city walls — it's the first thing you should do, ideally early morning before the cruise ship passengers arrive. The full circuit is about 2km and takes 90 minutes with stops. Start from the Pile Gate entrance, go counter-clockwise, and you'll hit the best views over the Old Town rooftops and out to Lokrum island on the less crowded stretch first. Bring water — there's almost no shade and it gets properly hot from June onwards.

Take the ferry to Lokrum island (10 minutes from the Old Port, boats run every half hour). It's a nature reserve with pine forests, swimming spots off the rocks, a ruined Benedictine monastery, and a Dead Sea — a small saltwater lake in the middle of the island that's warm enough to float in. Pack a picnic because the on-island café is overpriced and limited. The last ferry back is around 5pm, so don't lose track of time.

## The Vibe

Dubrovnik is undeniably beautiful but it's also a city dealing with overtourism, and you'll feel that tension. On days when multiple cruise ships dock, the Stradun (main street) becomes a shuffle. Time your Old Town visits for early morning or late evening and you'll have a completely different experience — the stone streets practically glow in the low light, and the bars along the walls come alive without the daytime crowds.

Outside the walls, the Lapad and Babin Kuk areas are where the locals actually live. There's a more relaxed beach culture out there, and the restaurants are cheaper and often better than the tourist traps inside the gates. The Gruž harbour area has a morning market that's worth the walk — fresh fruit, lavender sachets, olive oil, and local cheese.

## Things to See

Beyond the walls walk, the cable car up to Mount Srđ gives you the panoramic view that's on every postcard. At the top there's a small museum about the 1991 siege of Dubrovnik — sobering and important context for understanding the city beyond its photogenic surface. The Fort Lovrijenac (St Lawrence Fortress) just outside the western wall is the "Red Keep" from Game of Thrones and offers excellent views back along the walls.

Inside the Old Town, the Rector's Palace is the best museum — it covers Dubrovnik's history as an independent republic and the building itself is worth seeing. The Franciscan Monastery on the Stradun has one of Europe's oldest pharmacies (dating to 1317) and a peaceful cloister garden. For beaches, Banje Beach just east of the Old Port is the closest, but Sveti Jakov beach a 20-minute walk south is far better — fewer people, clearer water, and a spectacular view back to the city walls.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Dubrovnik is relatively safe. Croatian cuisine is Mediterranean-influenced — olive oil, grilled fish, grilled meats, fresh vegetables — and nuts are not a significant ingredient in savoury dishes. The Dalmatian coast specialises in simply grilled seafood, peka (slow-cooked meat and vegetables under a domed lid), and black risotto (squid ink). None of these typically involve nuts. The main caution is desserts: orahnjača (walnut roll) and some cakes use ground walnuts. Also check any pesto-style sauces, as pine nuts may appear. "Imam alergiju na orašaste plodove" covers it, but English is widely spoken in Dubrovnik's tourist areas and you'll have no trouble communicating.

For vegetarians, Dubrovnik is a challenge. Dalmatian cuisine is built around seafood and grilled meats, and traditional restaurants may have only a šopska salad (tomato, cucumber, onion, feta) or grilled vegetables as meat-free options. Pasta dishes are available everywhere but tend to be afterthoughts. Your best bets: Nishta in the Old Town is a dedicated vegetarian restaurant with creative Mediterranean dishes. Azur does excellent Asian-fusion including vegetarian options. For quick meals, bakeries throughout the Old Town sell burek (filled pastry — cheese versions are good) and pizza by the slice. If you're staying in an apartment, the Gruž market has excellent fresh produce for self-catering.
