## Things to Do

Cross Charles Bridge before 8am if you can drag yourselves out of bed — by mid-morning it's a solid wall of tour groups and selfie sticks. The bridge is genuinely beautiful at dawn when the mist comes off the Vltava and you can actually see the statues without someone's head in the way. From the bridge, walk up to Prague Castle (Hradčany) — the complex is enormous and includes St Vitus Cathedral, which has some of the best stained glass you'll see anywhere, including a Mucha window that's worth the visit alone.

Spend an afternoon in the Letná Park beer garden, which sits on a hill above the river with panoramic views over the old town and all its spires. The beer is cheap even by Prague standards and the atmosphere is completely local — families, dog walkers, students. If you want to explore further, walk from Letná through Stromovka park, Prague's largest, which feels like actual countryside ten minutes from the centre.

## The Vibe

Prague has two faces. The Old Town and Charles Bridge area is tourist-heavy and can feel like a theme park — overpriced restaurants with hawkers outside, Segway tours blocking pavements, and shops selling identical marionettes. Step two streets away from the main drag and it transforms completely. Žižkov, the neighbourhood east of the centre, is full of cheap pubs where a half-litre of excellent Pilsner costs about 50p and nobody speaks English. Vinohrady, just south, is the more polished version — tree-lined streets, good coffee, wine bars, and a slightly older crowd.

The city punches well above its weight for nightlife. There's a cluster of underground bars in the streets behind Old Town Square — Hemingway Bar does proper cocktails in a low-ceilinged brick vault. Cross the river for the Smíchov area where the old industrial spaces have been converted into bars and clubs. Prague is absurdly affordable for going out — you'll spend less on a full evening here than on two rounds in London.

## Things to See

Old Town Square is the obvious starting point — the Astronomical Clock does its thing on the hour and it's worth seeing once, though it's over in about 45 seconds and you'll wonder what the crowd was about. The real draw of the square is the architecture: Týn Church with its gothic spires, the baroque St Nicholas Church, and the art nouveau buildings around the edges. Walk through the Jewish Quarter (Josefov) nearby — the Old Jewish Cemetery, with its layers of headstones stacked over centuries, is quietly one of the most moving things in the city.

Take the funicular up Petřín Hill for the best views of Prague without the crowds at the castle. There's a miniature Eiffel Tower at the top (Prague's answer to the real thing, built two years after the original) and a mirror maze that's genuinely entertaining after a few beers. On the way back down, the Strahov Monastery has a magnificent baroque library hall and also brews its own beer — the amber St Norbert is excellent.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Prague is one of the easier European destinations. Czech cooking is built around meat, bread dumplings, sauces, and potatoes — nuts simply don't feature in the traditional repertoire. The classic pub dishes — svíčková (beef in cream sauce with dumplings), vepřo-knedlo-zelo (pork with dumplings and sauerkraut), guláš — are all nut-free. Street food like trdelník is usually safe but the toppings vary between vendors, so ask if there are nuts in the coating or filling. Langos (fried dough with cheese) is another safe street food option.

EU allergen labelling law means restaurants must identify the 14 major allergens including tree nuts and peanuts. In practice, the fancier restaurants and modern cafés do this well, while the old-school pubs may need you to ask directly. Most staff in central Prague speak enough English to handle allergen questions, but a Czech-language allergy card doesn't hurt in more local places. Bakeries are the main area of caution — Czech cakes and koláče (filled pastries) sometimes contain walnuts.

For vegetarians, the honest answer is that traditional Czech food isn't your friend. The menus in tourist-area pubs will list smažený sýr (deep-fried Edam) as the sole veggie option, and it's basically a slab of fried cheese with chips. That said, Prague has moved on enormously. In Vinohrady, try Etnosvět for globally-inspired vegetarian food, or Dhaba Beas for cheap Indian buffet lunches that are entirely vegetarian. Karlín, the up-and-coming district across the river, has several modern restaurants with strong veggie menus. The farmers' markets at Náplavka on the riverbank (Saturdays) are worth a visit for fresh produce and cooked food stalls.
