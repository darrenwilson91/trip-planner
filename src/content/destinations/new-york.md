## Things to Do

Walk the High Line from Gansevoort Street up to Hudson Yards — it's a converted elevated railway turned into a park, and the stretch through Chelsea gives you this strange feeling of floating above the city while gardens and art installations drift past. Go early morning on a weekday if you can; by noon at weekends it's shoulder-to-shoulder. At the north end, the Vessel and Edge observation deck at Hudson Yards are worth a look, though Edge is properly expensive (about $40 each).

Cross the Brooklyn Bridge on foot — start from the Manhattan side for the best views unfolding ahead of you, then reward yourself in Dumbo on the other side. Time Out Market New York is right there under the bridge for lunch, or walk up to Juliana's Pizza on Old Fulton Street (not Grimaldi's next door — Juliana's is the original). From Dumbo, the Brooklyn Heights Promenade is a five-minute walk and gives you that classic postcard Manhattan skyline view.

Spend a morning at Chelsea Market — it's a food hall in a former Nabisco factory on Ninth Avenue. Los Tacos No. 1 does excellent corn tortilla tacos, and the Doughnuttery sells tiny sugared doughnuts that are absurdly good. The market connects to the High Line, so you can combine them. For a different food hall experience, head to Essex Market on the Lower East Side — it's less touristy and the Shopsin's counter is genuinely chaotic in the best way.

## The Vibe

New York is relentless in the way only New York is. Every block has something happening — someone arguing with a parking meter, a jazz trio playing in a subway station, a queue of forty people outside a ramen shop the size of a cupboard. It's exhausting and exhilarating in equal measure. The key is to pick a neighbourhood each day and let yourself get lost in it rather than trying to tick off every landmark.

Greenwich Village and the West Village feel like a different city from Midtown — tree-lined streets, brownstone townhouses, independent bookshops (Three Lives & Company on West 10th is lovely), and Italian restaurants that have been there since your grandparents were young. The East Village and Lower East Side are scrappier, with punk history, dive bars, and some of the best cheap food in Manhattan. Williamsburg across the river in Brooklyn has calmed down from its peak hipster era but still has excellent coffee (Devoción on Grand Street), record shops, and Domino Park on the waterfront.

Midtown is where the big stuff lives — Times Square (see it once, then avoid it), the Met on Fifth Avenue (pay what you wish for NY residents, set price for visitors — budget a whole day), MoMA on 53rd Street, and the Theatre District if you want a Broadway show. TKTS in Times Square sells same-day discounted tickets, but the line is long — the TKTS booth at Lincoln Center is much quieter.

## Things to See

Central Park deserves more time than most people give it. Beyond the obvious Bethesda Fountain and Bow Bridge, head to the Ramble — a deliberately wild, wooded section in the middle of the park that feels impossibly far from Manhattan. The Conservatory Garden at 105th Street is formal and beautiful and almost always quiet. Rent a rowboat at the Loeb Boathouse if the weather's good.

The Metropolitan Museum of Art is staggering — the Temple of Dendur in the Egyptian wing, the rooftop garden with its Manhattan views, the American Wing's Tiffany glass. You genuinely cannot see it in one visit, so pick two or three departments and commit. The Cloisters, the Met's medieval branch up in Fort Tryon Park, is one of the most underrated museums in the city — a rebuilt medieval monastery overlooking the Hudson River with unicorn tapestries and herb gardens.

For something different, take the Staten Island Ferry — it's free, it runs every 30 minutes, and it passes right by the Statue of Liberty. You don't need to buy a ticket for the statue itself unless you want to go inside (and honestly, the ferry view is better). Grand Central Terminal is worth a wander for the ceiling alone — the zodiac mural is painted backwards, which nobody noticed for decades.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

New York is one of the safer major cities for nut allergies, largely because the food culture takes dietary requirements seriously. The US doesn't have allergen labelling laws as strict as the UK's, but New York City restaurants are culturally very accommodating — staff will check with the kitchen without making you feel difficult about it. Most mid-range and upscale restaurants can provide ingredient lists on request.

The areas to be cautious are the same as anywhere: bakeries (New York's incredible bakery scene means almond flour, hazelnut praline, and pistachio everything are everywhere — Levain Bakery, Dominique Ansel, Breads Bakery all use tree nuts extensively) and Asian restaurants. Chinatown in particular — the language barrier in traditional Cantonese restaurants can make allergen communication tricky, and wok-fried dishes often use peanut oil or cashews. Thai and Korean restaurants also use peanuts and sesame heavily. Japanese restaurants (sushi, ramen) are generally safer on the nut front.

For vegetarians, New York is genuinely extraordinary. Dedicated spots worth booking: Dirt Candy on the Lower East Side (creative vegetable-forward tasting menus), Superiority Burger in the East Village (the impossible-to-categorise veggie burger and rotating sides), and Avant Garden in the East Village for upscale vegan. But honestly, you don't need specialist restaurants — a plain cheese slice from any corner pizza shop is one of the best things you'll eat, and the falafel from Mamoun's on MacDougal Street has been perfect since 1971. Indian restaurants along Lexington Avenue in the upper 20s (Curry Hill) do excellent vegetarian thalis, and Xi'an Famous Foods does hand-pulled noodle dishes where the vegetables are genuinely the star.
