## Things to Do

Start at Torvehallerne, the covered food market next to Nørreport station. It's two glass halls full of food stalls — Danish cheese, fresh pastries, smørrebrød, coffee. Grab a cardamom bun from Laura's Bakery and a flat white from Coffee Collective, then wander. It's not cheap (nothing in Copenhagen is) but the quality is consistently excellent.

Rent a bike — Copenhagen was built for cycling and the infrastructure is incredible. Wide, separated bike lanes, traffic lights specifically for cyclists, and a flat city that makes it effortless. Cycle along the harbour from Nyhavn down to the Copenhagen Contemporary art space, then across the Cykelslangen (bicycle snake bridge) to Vesterbro for lunch. Tivoli Gardens is worth an evening visit even if you don't ride anything — the gardens and architecture are genuinely beautiful, especially when the lights come on at dusk.

## The Vibe

Copenhagen radiates a kind of calm competence — everything works, everything is well-designed, and nobody seems particularly stressed about anything. Hygge is a real thing here, not just a marketing concept. You feel it in the candlelit restaurants, the communal seating at food halls, the way people linger over coffee.

Nørrebro is the most diverse neighbourhood and has the best street food scene. Vesterbro has gone from gritty to gentrified but still has character, with Meatpacking District restaurants and Istedgade's mix of old and new. The harbour area around Islands Brygge has a public swimming pool literally in the harbour — Danes swimming in clean city water pretty much sums up how this city operates.

## Things to See

The Louisiana Museum of Modern Art is about 35 minutes north by train and it's world-class — the sculpture garden overlooking the Øresund strait is extraordinary. Back in the city, the Designmuseum Danmark is essential if you're interested in why Danish furniture and product design conquered the world. The National Museum of Denmark is free and covers Viking history better than anywhere else.

Christiania, the self-proclaimed free town in Christianshavn, is worth a walk through — it started as a squatter commune in 1971 and has its own rules and atmosphere. The architecture ranges from hand-built wooden houses to converted military barracks. It's fascinating and a bit surreal. The Church of Our Saviour nearby has a spiralling external staircase to the top of its spire — the 360-degree views of Copenhagen are spectacular, though the last stretch is not for anyone uneasy with heights.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Copenhagen is one of the safest cities in Europe. Danish food culture rarely uses nuts — smørrebrød (open sandwiches), frikadeller (meatballs), stegt flæsk (crispy pork), and most traditional dishes are completely nut-free. The New Nordic cuisine movement focuses on local, seasonal ingredients and nuts are rarely central to dishes. Danish allergen law requires restaurants to provide allergen information, and staff take it genuinely seriously — you won't get an eye-roll when you ask about ingredients. Bakeries are the one area to check, as some Danish pastries may contain marzipan (almond paste), but they'll tell you clearly.

For vegetarians, the modern Copenhagen food scene delivers. Places like Souls (vegan street food), SimpleRaw, and Ark do excellent plant-based cooking. The New Nordic approach means seasonal vegetables are often the star of the dish even in non-vegetarian restaurants. Torvehallerne has several vegetarian stalls. Traditional Danish food is harder — classic smørrebrød is mostly herring, beef, and pork — but honestly, you're unlikely to eat at many traditional places when the contemporary scene is this good. Budget option: falafel from the Nørrebro strip along Nørrebrogade is consistently brilliant and cheap by Copenhagen standards.
