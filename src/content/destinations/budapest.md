## Things to Do

Budapest is really two cities split by the Danube — Buda on the hilly west bank and Pest on the flat east side — and the best way to get your bearings is to walk across the Chain Bridge at sunset. Start on the Pest side, cross over, and climb up to the Fisherman's Bastion for that classic panoramic view back across the river to Parliament. It's touristy, yes, but the view genuinely earns it.

The thermal baths are non-negotiable. Széchenyi is the famous one — a massive yellow neo-baroque complex in City Park where you can soak in 38°C water while chess players concentrate on floating boards around you. If you want something less crowded, try Rudas Baths on the Buda side, which has an Ottoman-era octagonal pool and a rooftop pool with Danube views. Go on a weekday morning to avoid the hen-do crowds.

The ruin bar scene is what makes Budapest's nightlife unlike anywhere else. Szimpla Kert is the original — a crumbling old apartment building turned into a labyrinth of mismatched furniture, bathtubs, and art installations. It gets rammed after 10pm, so go for an afternoon drink or the Sunday morning farmers' market they host. Élesztő on Tűzoltó utca is better for craft beer in a more relaxed setting.

## The Vibe

Budapest has an energy that feels like Berlin did ten years ago — slightly rough around the edges, creative, and absurdly cheap by European standards. You can eat a proper sit-down dinner with wine for under £20 a head, which still catches us off guard coming from London prices. The city has a confidence about it that's different from the self-consciousness you sometimes get in other Central European capitals trying to prove themselves.

District VII (the Jewish Quarter) is where most of the action happens — ruin bars, street food, vintage shops, and a genuine community underneath the tourist layer. Walk down Kazinczy utca and Dob utca on a weekday afternoon and you'll find local coffee shops and bookshops between the party venues. Across the river, the Castle District in Buda is quieter and more polished, good for a morning wander and lunch away from the Pest crowds.

## Things to See

The Parliament Building is one of Europe's most impressive — the neo-Gothic exterior is dramatic enough from the outside, but the interior tour is worth booking ahead for the main staircase alone. The Hungarian National Gallery in the Royal Palace on Castle Hill has an excellent collection spanning medieval altarpieces to twentieth-century Hungarian art, and admission is reasonable.

Central Market Hall (Nagy Vásárcsarnok) on Fővám tér is the place to go for paprika, pick sausages, and lángos from the upper-floor food stalls. The ground floor is a proper working market with fruit, vegetables, and meat — go in the morning when it's locals shopping rather than tour groups. For something different, take the HÉV suburban train from Batthyány tér out to Szentendre, a small artists' town on a bend in the Danube, about 40 minutes each way and a good half-day escape.

Margaret Island, in the middle of the Danube between Margaret Bridge and Árpád Bridge, is where locals go to run, swim, and sit on the grass away from traffic. It's entirely car-free and has an outdoor pool complex, a small Japanese garden, and a medieval ruin or two. Perfect for a lazy afternoon when you've had enough of the inner city.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Budapest needs moderate caution — mainly around desserts and baked goods. Hungarian patisseries (cukrászdák) are landmines: Dobos torte layers, walnut strudel (diós rétes), and the poppy seed rolls that appear everywhere all contain nuts or are made alongside nut products. The Gerbeaud café on Vörösmarty tér is gorgeous but nearly everything in the cake cabinet has nuts. For savoury food, goulash, chicken paprikash (ask if their version includes ground walnuts — some do, many don't), lángos, and most street food are fine. The Hungarian phrase is "dióra allergiás vagyok" (I am allergic to nuts) — write it down and show it to servers.

Vegetarians will manage but should set expectations. The Jewish Quarter restaurants like Mazel Tov and Kőleves have proper vegetarian sections, not just a token salad. Főzelék bars serve these thick, traditional Hungarian vegetable stews — spinach, pea, potato — that are genuinely good and filling. Hummus Bar on Október 6. utca does reliable Middle Eastern vegetarian food when you need a break from Hungarian cuisine. Outside the central tourist areas, menus get much more meat-focused, so stick to Districts V, VI, and VII for the best options.
