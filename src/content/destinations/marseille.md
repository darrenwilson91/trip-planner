## Things to Do

Marseille is not a city that performs for tourists — it earns your attention through sheer character. Start at the Vieux Port early in the morning when the fishermen are selling the day's catch directly from their boats along the quai des Belges. The fish market runs until about 1pm and it's the real thing — no one's here for Instagram, they're buying dinner. From the port, take the land train or walk the steep path up to Notre-Dame de la Garde, the basilica that watches over the entire city. The views from the top cover the harbour, the Frioul islands, and on a clear day you can see as far as the Calanques to the southeast.

The Calanques themselves are the main draw if you like the outdoors. These dramatic limestone inlets between Marseille and Cassis have water so clear it looks artificially blue. The most accessible is Calanque de Sugiton, reached by a 45-minute walk from the Luminy bus terminus. For something more challenging, the trail from Callelongue to Marseilleveyre takes about three hours round trip and rewards you with total solitude. Arrive early in summer — they close the trails when fire risk is high, and by 10am the car parks are full. If hiking isn't your thing, boats run from the Vieux Port to the three main calanques and cost around €25 return.

## The Vibe

Marseille has more in common with Naples or Algiers than it does with Lyon or Bordeaux. It's France's oldest city, its biggest port, and its most diverse. The energy is Mediterranean in the truest sense — loud, direct, occasionally chaotic, always authentic. This is not a city that's been polished for weekend visitors. The graffiti is everywhere, the traffic is assertive, and the accent is thick enough that Parisians joke they need subtitles.

Le Panier, the old town above the port, is where Marseille's character is most concentrated. The streets are narrow and steep, the buildings are painted in ochre and terracotta, and every corner has either a street artist's mural or someone's grandmother hanging washing from a window. Cours Julien, a ten-minute walk south, is the creative quarter — record shops, tattoo parlours, independent bookshops, and bars with chairs spilling across the pavement. Friday and Saturday nights here have a loose, improvised quality that feels entirely un-curated. La Plaine market on Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday mornings is worth the early start for the North African spices, stacked olives, and excellent socca.

## Things to See

MuCEM — the Museum of European and Mediterranean Civilisations — is Marseille's cultural flagship. The building itself is extraordinary: a concrete cube wrapped in a latticed shell that casts geometric shadows across the walkways. It sits at the mouth of the Vieux Port on the old J4 pier, connected by a dramatic footbridge to Fort Saint-Jean, a 17th-century fortification with gardens and harbour views. The permanent collection traces Mediterranean civilisation through objects, film, and art. Even if museums aren't usually your thing, the architecture and setting are worth the visit alone.

The Frioul archipelago is a 20-minute ferry from the Vieux Port. Château d'If — the island prison from *The Count of Monte Cristo* — gets the most visitors, but the neighbouring island of Ratonneau is better for an afternoon: swimming coves with no crowds, crumbling military ruins to explore, and a few food shacks serving grilled fish. Ferries run roughly every hour and cost about €10 return. Back on the mainland, the Vallon des Auffes is a tiny fishing port tucked beneath the Corniche Kennedy coast road — blink and you'll miss the turning. It's barely changed in fifty years: a handful of pointu boats, a restaurant or two built into the rock, and the sound of waves hitting the seawall. Chez Fonfon here does what might be the best bouillabaisse in the city, though you'll pay handsomely for it.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

The nut situation in Marseille mirrors broader French cooking. Patisseries are high-risk territory — almost everything behind the glass will contain almonds, hazelnuts, or pistachios in some form. Financiers are pure almond. Macarons use almond flour as their base. Galette des rois has frangipane. If you're buying from a boulangerie, ask clearly: "Est-ce qu'il y a des fruits à coque?" (Are there tree nuts?). Savoury Provençal food is generally safer. Bouillabaisse and most fish dishes are nut-free. Ratatouille, socca, and panisse (fried chickpea chips) are all safe. Watch for pistou — the Provençal cousin of pesto — which contains pine nuts and appears in soupe au pistou, a summer staple. Some Provençal sauces use ground almonds as a thickener, so always ask.

For vegetarians, Marseille is honest rather than easy. The city's food identity is built on fish and seafood. The classic dishes — bouillabaisse, navettes, pieds et paquets — are all animal-based. That said, the North African and Middle Eastern influence means you'll find couscous with vegetables, falafel, hummus, and stuffed aubergines around Noailles market and Cours Julien. Socca and panisse from street vendors are reliably filling. In Le Panier and the streets around La Friche la Belle de Mai, newer restaurants offer more creative vegetarian cooking. La Plaine market is your best friend for assembling your own meals — the Provençal tomatoes, courgettes, and peppers in summer are outstanding and need almost nothing doing to them. Avoid the old-guard portside restaurants if you want more than chips and salad.
