## Things to Do

Start at Fushimi Inari Taisha as early as you can manage — the thousand vermillion torii gates are one of Japan's most photographed sights, and before 7am you'll have stretches of the trail almost to yourself. The full hike to the summit takes about two hours and rewards you with views over the city, but even the first twenty minutes through the densest gate tunnel is worth the early alarm. Come back down and grab onigiri from a convenience store for breakfast — you'll have earned it.

Arashiyama deserves a half-day at minimum. The bamboo grove gets the Instagram attention, but the real draw is the wider area: cross the Togetsukyo Bridge, rent a rowing boat on the Hozu River, and walk south to the Okochi Sanso villa gardens for one of the best views in all of Kyoto. If you're there on a weekday, the Sagano Romantic Train runs along the river gorge — touristy but genuinely scenic. Tenryu-ji temple at the grove entrance has one of the finest Zen gardens in Japan, and the rear garden exit drops you right into the bamboo.

## The Vibe

Kyoto moves at a completely different pace to Tokyo. Where Tokyo is neon and sensory overload, Kyoto is quiet stone paths, moss-covered temple grounds, and the sound of water trickling through bamboo pipes. The city takes its traditions seriously — you'll see maiko (apprentice geisha) walking through the Gion district in the early evening, wooden machiya townhouses converted into cafés and guest houses, and shops that have been selling the same pickles or incense for three hundred years.

The Higashiyama district running from Kiyomizu-dera down through Ninenzaka and Sannenzaka is the heart of old Kyoto — flagstone lanes lined with tea houses and ceramics shops. It's heavily touristed during the day but atmospheric in the early morning or after 5pm when the day-trippers head back to Osaka. For a more local feel, head to the Nishijin textile district in the northwest — the area around Daitoku-ji temple complex is residential, unhurried, and has some of the best sub-temples for quiet contemplation.

## Things to See

Kinkaku-ji (the Golden Pavilion) is the postcard shot — a three-storey gold-leaf pavilion reflected in its mirror pond. It's always crowded, but the garden circuit is one-way and well managed, so it never feels unpleasant. Ginkaku-ji (the Silver Pavilion) at the other end of the Philosopher's Path is less flashy but more interesting architecturally — the meticulously raked sand garden is mesmerising.

The Philosopher's Path itself is a two-kilometre canal-side walk connecting the two pavilions, lined with cherry trees that are extraordinary in late March and early April. In autumn, the maples along the route turn the walk into a tunnel of red and gold. Either season, allow time for detours — Honen-in temple just off the path is a hidden favourite with a thatched gate and moss garden.

Nijo Castle is different from anything else in Kyoto — a Tokugawa-era fortress with famous "nightingale floors" that chirp underfoot (an early intruder alarm). The palace interiors are decorated with stunning gold-leaf screen paintings. Nearby, the Imperial Palace grounds are open parkland and perfect for a quiet afternoon.

For something different, take the Keihan train thirty minutes south to Uji — the town produces Japan's finest matcha and has an excellent tea museum where you can grind your own. Byodo-in temple, the building on the back of the ten-yen coin, is worth the trip alone.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

The same warnings that apply to Tokyo hold here: **nut allergies require serious caution in Japan.** Sesame oil and sesame seeds are in practically everything — dressings, sauces, rice toppings, and many grilled dishes. Peanuts appear in some dipping sauces and as a garnish, often without being listed separately. Tree nuts show up in traditional wagashi sweets (walnut mochi, chestnut yokan) and in some savoury preparations you wouldn't expect. The language barrier is the biggest challenge — most restaurant staff outside tourist areas speak limited English, so a printed Japanese allergy card (アレルギーカード) listing your specific allergens is essential, not optional.

Your safest bets are the same as Tokyo: convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) label all allergens on packaged food with clear icons, and chain restaurants like CoCo Ichibanya (curry — choose plain toppings and check the menu's allergen chart) offer detailed allergen information. In Kyoto specifically, the higher-end kaiseki restaurants are more accustomed to dietary requests and will often work with you if you communicate in advance — some ryokan (traditional inns) will prepare allergen-free meals if you notify them at booking.

For vegetarians, Kyoto is genuinely better than most of Japan thanks to the Buddhist shojin ryori tradition. Temples like Tenryu-ji and Daitoku-ji have associated restaurants serving multi-course plant-based meals — no dashi, no meat, no fish. Shigetsu at Tenryu-ji is a good starting point: you eat on tatami mats overlooking the garden, and the food is seasonal, delicate, and entirely vegan. Outside temple restaurants, the same dashi problem persists — even seemingly vegetable dishes (miso soup, simmered vegetables, udon broth) almost certainly contain bonito fish stock unless you specifically confirm otherwise. Mumokuteki Cafe near Teramachi shopping street is a reliable vegetarian spot. The HappyCow app remains essential for finding options.
