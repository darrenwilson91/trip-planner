## Things to Do

Walk the length of La Rambla early in the morning before the crowds hit, then duck into La Boqueria market for fresh fruit juice and a plate of grilled vegetables. Spend a morning wandering the Gothic Quarter — the narrow medieval streets around Plaça del Rei are properly atmospheric, not just postcard material. If you want beaches, Barceloneta is the obvious one but head further north to Bogatell for fewer people and better sand.

Book Sagrada Familia tickets well in advance — this isn't optional, it's genuinely one of the most extraordinary buildings you'll ever walk into. The light through the stained glass in the afternoon is something else. Park Güell needs advance tickets too, but the free zone around it is honestly just as good for views over the city.

## The Vibe

Barcelona feels like a city that actually enjoys being alive. People eat dinner at 10pm, the beach is a 15-minute walk from the old town, and there's a casualness to everything that makes it impossible to feel stressed. It's big enough to have world-class restaurants and museums but compact enough to walk most of it.

The Eixample district is all wide avenues and Modernista architecture — good for a wander between lunch and dinner. El Born is where the locals drink, with tiny wine bars and craft beer spots tucked into old stone buildings. Gràcia, further up the hill, feels like its own village with independent shops and quiet squares.

## Things to See

The Sagrada Familia is the headline, obviously, but Casa Batlló on Passeig de Gràcia is arguably more impressive inside — the way Gaudí designed the light wells and windows is genuinely ingenious. The Picasso Museum in El Born is worth a couple of hours, especially the rooms showing his early Barcelona-period work.

For something different, take the funicular up to Montjuïc for the Fundació Joan Miró and panoramic views over the port. The MNAC (national art museum) at the top of the hill has an incredible Romanesque art collection and a terrace that overlooks the whole city. On Sundays, the fountain show at the bottom of the Montjuïc steps is free and genuinely spectacular.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Barcelona is relatively safe. Catalan cuisine doesn't rely heavily on nuts in main dishes. The two things to watch are romesco sauce (made with almonds and hazelnuts — it's served with calçots and sometimes grilled vegetables) and crema catalana or other desserts that may contain ground almonds. Bread, tapas, paella, and most rice dishes are fine. Staff in restaurants are generally helpful when you explain allergies — "tengo alergia a los frutos secos" gets the message across.

For vegetarians, this city is a dream. Beyond the dedicated vegetarian restaurants (Teresa Carles in the Raval is a classic), standard Catalan restaurants serve loads of meat-free dishes: escalivada (roasted aubergine and peppers), espinacs amb panses i pinyons (spinach with raisins and pine nuts — skip this one with nut allergies), patatas bravas, truita (Spanish omelette), and endless grilled vegetable platters. La Boqueria market has brilliant fresh produce stalls. You won't struggle here at all.
