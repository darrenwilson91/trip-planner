## Things to Do

Walk up to the Acropolis first thing in the morning — and by first thing, I mean be at the gate when it opens at 8am. By 10am the queue snakes down the hillside and the sun is hammering the exposed marble. The Parthenon is extraordinary up close, even wrapped in scaffolding as it perpetually seems to be. The views from the top across the city to the mountains are worth the climb alone. Don't skip the Acropolis Museum at the base of the hill — it's modern, beautifully laid out, and the glass floor section where you can see the archaeological excavation below your feet is genuinely clever.

Spend a morning wandering through the National Garden, the patch of green just behind the Parliament building on Syntagma Square. It's shady, quiet, and there are tortoises living in the ponds — an oddly charming thing to stumble across in a capital city. From there, walk down to the Panathenaic Stadium, the marble horseshoe where the first modern Olympics were held in 1896. You can walk on the actual track, which is a weirdly moving experience if you care about sport at all.

## The Vibe

Athens has a rawness that sets it apart from the polished European capitals. The graffiti is everywhere — on neoclassical mansions, on concrete apartment blocks, on shuttered shops. Some of it is genuinely impressive street art, some of it is political, and some of it is just tags. It gives the city an edge that you either love or find scrappy. The Exarcheia neighbourhood, the old anarchist quarter north of the centre, is the most concentrated version of this — independent bookshops, record stores, volunteer-run cafés, and murals covering entire building facades.

Plaka, the old neighbourhood directly below the Acropolis, is the tourist centre and it leans into it — souvenir shops, rooftop restaurants with Acropolis views, hawkers trying to seat you. It's pleasant enough for a wander, especially the quieter backstreets of Anafiotika where whitewashed houses look like they've been transplanted from a Cycladic island. The real Athens eating and drinking happens elsewhere though. Psyrri has a concentration of bars and tavernas in converted workshops. Koukaki, the residential neighbourhood south of the Acropolis, has a more local feel with excellent small restaurants.

## Things to See

The Acropolis dominates but the Ancient Agora below it is arguably more interesting as an experience — it's less crowded, beautifully planted with olive trees, and the reconstructed Stoa of Attalos gives you a tangible sense of what these spaces actually looked like when they were in use. The Temple of Hephaestus there is the best-preserved ancient Greek temple anywhere, with its columns and roof still intact.

Monastiraki flea market runs every day but Sunday is when it sprawls. The permanent shops sell mostly tourist tat and leather goods, but the Sunday street market is more interesting — old vinyl records, vintage cameras, brass coffee pots, and plenty of junk that's fun to rummage through. The square itself has a view straight up to the Acropolis that never gets old. From Monastiraki, walk through Ermou Street (the main shopping drag) or cut down to Kerameikos, the ancient cemetery district that's one of the most atmospheric and least visited archaeological sites in the centre.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

The main nut risk in Greek food is in desserts and pastries. Baklava, kataifi, and most of the syrup-soaked pastry family are built on layers of walnuts, pistachios, or almonds — avoid the entire pastry counter unless you've checked every item individually. Halva sometimes contains nuts too. On the savoury side, most traditional Greek cooking is nut-free — moussaka, souvlaki, grilled fish, Greek salad, and dips like tzatziki and taramasalata are all safe. The one to watch is skordalia (garlic dip), which in some regions is thickened with ground walnuts instead of bread or potato — always ask which version they make.

EU allergen regulations apply in Greece, and most restaurants in Athens have allergen information available if you ask. English is very widely spoken in the tourist areas and increasingly in the rest of the city too, so communicating your allergy is straightforward. Tavernas are generally happy to confirm ingredients — Greek hospitality means they'll usually check with the kitchen rather than guess. Pine nuts turn up in some salads and rice pilafs, so mention tree nuts specifically when ordering.

For vegetarians, Athens is one of the best Mediterranean cities. The mezze tradition means every taverna has a section of the menu dedicated to small vegetable and cheese dishes — you can order five or six and have a better meal than most meat eaters. Gigantes plaki (giant white beans slow-cooked in tomato sauce) is comfort food at its finest. Horta (boiled wild greens with lemon and olive oil) is on every menu and surprisingly good. Briam (Greek ratatouille) is another staple. In Exarcheia and Koukaki you'll find dedicated vegetarian and vegan restaurants, but honestly you won't need them — standard tavernas have so many naturally meat-free options that you'll eat brilliantly without trying.
