## Things to Do

Gamla Stan is the obvious starting point — the old town dates back to the 13th century, and the narrow cobbled streets around Stortorget (the main square) are genuinely atmospheric rather than just touristy. The Royal Palace sits at the north end, but the real draw is wandering the side streets: Prästgatan, Österlånggatan, and the tiny alley of Mårten Trotzigs Gränd, which narrows to less than a metre wide. Pop into Chokladkoppen on Stortorget for a hot chocolate, then cross the bridge to Riddarholmen for views back across the water.

The Vasa Museum on Djurgården is probably Stockholm's best single attraction — a fully intact 17th-century warship that sank on its maiden voyage and was pulled from the harbour 333 years later. It's genuinely jaw-dropping to see up close. While you're on Djurgården, the ABBA Museum is more fun than it has any right to be (even if you're not a fan), and the Skansen open-air museum gives a good overview of Swedish history and traditions. You could easily spend a full day on this island alone.

## The Vibe

Stockholm feels spacious in a way that most European capitals don't. The water is everywhere — between the islands, along the quaysides, opening up into the archipelago to the east. The light in summer is extraordinary, with the sun barely setting and a golden glow that lasts for hours. People eat outside whenever possible, and the city takes on an almost Mediterranean feel from June through August, with everyone gathered along Södermalm's cliffs at Skinnarviksberget watching the sunset over Kungsholmen.

There's a quiet confidence to Stockholm. The design aesthetic runs through everything — the metro stations are carved from raw rock and decorated by different artists (Kungsträdgården and T-Centralen are the standouts), the coffee is taken very seriously (Koppi, Drop Coffee, and Johan & Nyström are the places to try), and even the everyday architecture is considered. Södermalm is the neighbourhood with the most personality — independent shops along Götgatan, vintage stores around Nytorget, and a genuinely local feel that hasn't been over-touristed. SoFo (south of Folkungagatan) is particularly good for browsing.

## Things to See

Fotografiska, the photography museum on Södermalm's waterfront, is consistently excellent — the exhibitions rotate regularly and the top-floor restaurant has some of the best views in the city. The Moderna Museet on Skeppsholmen has a strong permanent collection of 20th-century art and a peaceful sculpture garden. For something different, take the ferry out to one of the archipelago islands — Vaxholm is the closest and most accessible, about an hour each way, with a fortress and waterfront cafés.

Walk or cycle along Strandvägen, the grand boulevard on Östermalm, then cut through the Östermalm Saluhall (the renovated indoor food hall) for lunch. It's pricier than Södermalm but the quality is excellent. The City Hall (Stadshuset) is worth visiting for the guided tour — the Blue Hall hosts the Nobel Prize banquet, and the Golden Hall's mosaics are stunning. In the evening, Hornstull in southern Södermalm has a good cluster of bars and restaurants that feel less polished and more relaxed than the Östermalm scene.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Sweden is one of the safest countries in Europe for nut allergies. Allergen labelling is taken seriously across the board — restaurants clearly mark dishes containing common allergens, and staff are trained to handle allergy conversations without fuss. Swedish savoury cooking almost never involves nuts. The main area to watch is bakeries and desserts: kanelbullar (cinnamon buns) are usually safe, but some Swedish cakes and pastries use almond flour or marzipan (prinsesstårta, the classic green marzipan cake, is a notable one). Just ask, and you'll get a clear, honest answer.

Vegetarians will eat well in Stockholm. Södermalm in particular has a strong plant-based culture — Hermans on Fjällgatan does an all-vegetarian buffet with panoramic harbour views, and there are dedicated vegan spots scattered across the neighbourhood. Most modern Swedish restaurants offer at least two or three vegetarian mains that feel intentional rather than token. The traditional Swedish table (husmanskost) revolves around meatballs, herring, and salmon, so you'll want to stick to contemporary restaurants rather than old-school Swedish places. The Östermalm Saluhall and Hötorgshallen food halls both have good vegetarian stall options for lunch.
