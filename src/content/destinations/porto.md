## Things to Do

Walk across the upper deck of the Dom Luís I bridge first thing in the morning before the crowds arrive. The views up and down the Douro are the defining image of Porto — terracotta rooftops stacked up the hillside on one side, port wine lodges on the other. Cross over to Vila Nova de Gaia and spend a couple of hours doing port tastings. Taylor's has the best views from their terrace, but Graham's and Sandeman are equally good on the tasting front. Pace yourself — vintage tawny at 11am hits different than you'd expect.

Back on the Porto side, Bolhão Market reopened after a long renovation and it's worth a proper visit. This isn't a tourist market — it's where locals buy fruit, cheese, dried cod, and flowers. The upper floor has food stalls if you want to eat there, but the ground floor is the real draw. Get some fresh bread and queijo da serra (mountain cheese) and have a picnic down by the river. On Saturdays, the flea market at Vandoma near the Sé cathedral has genuinely interesting finds — old tiles, vintage port bottles, battered furniture.

## The Vibe

Porto has always lived in Lisbon's shadow and has a bit of a chip on its shoulder about it, which honestly makes it more likeable. The city calls itself the "Invicta" (the unconquered) and there's a directness to people here that you don't get in Lisbon's slightly more polished atmosphere. It's a working city that happens to be beautiful, not a beautiful city that happens to still work.

The Ribeira waterfront is gorgeous but it's also the most touristy stretch — walk ten minutes uphill into Vitória or Cedofeita and you're in proper neighbourhood territory. Rua de Miguel Bombarda is the art gallery street, with a monthly open-gallery night that fills the surrounding bars. The Bonfim neighbourhood east of the centre is where a lot of the younger creative scene has settled — record shops, small-batch coffee roasters, wine bars that take natural wine very seriously.

## Things to See

São Bento station isn't just a transport hub — the entrance hall is covered floor to ceiling in around 20,000 hand-painted azulejo tiles depicting Portuguese history. It's one of the most photographed interiors in the country and it's completely free. Just walk in. The tiles date from the early 1900s and the battle scenes and historical moments are genuinely impressive craftsmanship.

Livraria Lello gets all the press (it supposedly inspired the Hogwarts staircase, though J.K. Rowling has been vague about this), and while the interior is stunning with its crimson neo-Gothic woodwork, the ticket queue can be painful. If you want to see it, go first thing on a weekday. More rewarding, honestly, is the Serralves Museum and its surrounding gardens on the western edge of the city — a proper contemporary art museum in an Art Deco villa with 18 hectares of parkland. The Clérigos Tower gives you a panoramic view over the city if you can manage the 240 steps.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Nut allergies are well-managed in Porto. Portuguese savoury cooking barely touches nuts — the francesinha (Porto's signature sandwich), bacalhau à Gomes de Sá, arroz de pato (duck rice), and most grilled fish dishes are all safe. The concern is pastries: Portuguese confectionery has a strong almond tradition, and many of the egg-based convent sweets include ground almonds. Pastéis de nata are nut-free, so you can eat those without worry. "Tenho alergia a frutos secos" will communicate your allergy — Porto restaurant staff are generally helpful, particularly in the Cedofeita, Foz, and Boavista areas where newer restaurants are more attuned to dietary requirements.

Vegetarian eating in Porto requires more planning than in Lisbon. The city's traditional food identity revolves around the francesinha, tripas à moda do Porto (tripe stew — they're literally nicknamed "tripe eaters"), and grilled fish. However, the Cedofeita neighbourhood has several dedicated vegetarian and vegan restaurants — daTerra does a popular buffet, and newer spots along Rua de Cedofeita and in the Bonfim area offer creative plant-based menus. For traditional restaurants, your best options are usually caldo verde (check it's chorizo-free), pão com queijo (bread with cheese), and whatever vegetables they're serving as sides. The food at Mercado do Bolhão gives you more control — buy fresh produce and local cheese and sort yourself out.
