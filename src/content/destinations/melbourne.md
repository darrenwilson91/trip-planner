## Things to Do

Start with coffee — this is non-negotiable. Melbourne's café culture isn't a marketing pitch; it's genuinely how the city operates. Skip the chains and find a hole-in-the-wall roaster in any inner suburb. Market Lane in the Queen Victoria Market precinct or Patricia Coffee Brewers on Little Bourke Street (standing room only, no Wi-Fi, intentionally no distractions) are good starting points. Order a flat white and don't ask for a large — you'll get a look.

The laneway culture is the other defining Melbourne experience. Hosier Lane gets the Instagram crowds, but the better street art is on Blender Lane, AC/DC Lane, and Croft Alley. Just walk — the city grid is designed for discovering things between buildings. Centre Place and Degraves Street are where laneways meet espresso bars, and they're worth the cliché.

Take the tram to South Melbourne Market on a Saturday morning. It's less touristy than Queen Vic and the dim sims from South Melbourne Dim Sims are a local institution. The free City Circle tram (Route 35) is a handy way to orient yourself on day one — it loops past Flinders Street Station, Federation Square, Docklands, and the Parliament end of town.

## The Vibe

Melbourne operates on a different frequency to Sydney. There's no harbour bridge or opera house demanding your attention — instead, the city reveals itself in layers. A converted warehouse hosting a natural wine bar. A Japanese kissaten hidden above a shoe shop on Little Collins Street. A record store that also serves dumplings. The city has a deep streak of "why not?" running through it.

The neighbourhoods each have their own personality. Fitzroy is where Melbourne's counterculture lives — Brunswick Street for vintage shops, Gertrude Street for galleries and small-batch everything. Carlton has the old Italian influence (Lygon Street, though locals will tell you the best Italian food moved to Northcote years ago). Richmond is the Vietnamese food corridor along Victoria Street — pho at 10am is completely normal there.

Weather-wise, the "four seasons in one day" joke is real. Pack layers even in summer. January and February can hit 40°C then drop to 18°C by evening. Melburnians talk about weather the way Londoners do, except with genuine grievance.

## Things to See

The NGV (National Gallery of Victoria) on St Kilda Road is Australia's oldest public art gallery and it's free for the permanent collection. The NGV International building itself — with its water wall entrance — is worth seeing even if you skip the exhibitions. Across the river, NGV Australia at Federation Square focuses on Australian and Indigenous art.

The Royal Botanic Gardens are genuinely beautiful, not just a park with a label. Walk from the Ornamental Lake through the fern gully to the Ian Potter Foundation Children's Garden area (good even without children — the design is clever). On a warm evening, the outdoor cinema in the gardens runs through summer.

For a day trip, the Great Ocean Road is the obvious choice. It's about a four-hour round trip to the Twelve Apostles, but the real highlights are the smaller stops — Lorne for a swim, Apollo Bay for lunch, the Otway Rainforest for a walk among giant ferns. Hire a car and leave early. Alternatively, the Yarra Valley wine region is only an hour east — De Bortoli and TarraWarra Estate for wine, plus the Healesville Sanctuary if you want to see platypuses and wombats in a non-zoo setting.

Brighton Beach and its painted bathing boxes are a half-hour tram ride from the city. Go for the photo, stay for a swim if the weather cooperates. St Kilda is nearby — the foreshore walk at dusk sometimes turns up little penguins returning to their nests along the breakwater.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Australia's Food Standards Code requires all food businesses to declare the presence of peanuts, tree nuts, and eight other major allergens. This isn't optional or best-practice — it's law. In practice, Melbourne cafés and restaurants are some of the most allergy-aware you'll encounter anywhere. Staff routinely ask about dietary requirements, and kitchens are accustomed to modifying dishes.

The multicultural food scene means you'll encounter cuisines that traditionally use nuts — Thai, Indian, Chinese, Middle Eastern. But the Australian versions of these restaurants operate under the same labelling laws, and the cultural expectation of allergen awareness is strong. You won't need to avoid any particular cuisine entirely; just mention the allergy when ordering and you'll be taken seriously.

For vegetarians and vegans, Melbourne is extraordinary. Smith & Daughters in Fitzroy (Italian-inspired, entirely vegan, doesn't feel like a compromise), Transformer in Fitzroy (share plates, seasonal), and Lentil As Anything (pay-what-you-feel community kitchens in Abbotsford and St Kilda) are standouts. But honestly, almost every café in the inner suburbs will have multiple vegetarian options on the menu without you having to ask. Brunch culture here has always leaned heavily on eggs, avocado, grains, and greens — meat is often the afterthought, not the centrepiece.
