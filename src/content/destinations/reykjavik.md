## Things to Do

The Golden Circle is the obvious day trip and it earns its reputation — Þingvellir National Park (where the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates are pulling apart, and where the Icelandic parliament was founded in 930 AD), Geysir (the original geyser — Strokkur erupts every 5-10 minutes), and Gullfoss waterfall (two tiers of glacial water thundering into a canyon). You can drive it yourself in a day or take a tour. Driving gives you more flexibility to stop at the smaller spots — the Friðheimar tomato farm does a surprisingly good tomato soup lunch.

The Blue Lagoon gets a lot of eye-rolling from travel snobs but it's genuinely enjoyable — silky blue geothermal water, steam rising around you, a drink from the swim-up bar. Book well in advance and go for an early morning slot to avoid the worst crowds. For a cheaper and less touristy alternative, the Sky Lagoon on the outskirts of Reykjavik opened recently and has infinity-edge views over the North Atlantic.

## The Vibe

Reykjavik is the smallest capital city you'll ever visit — the entire greater area has about 230,000 people and the centre is basically one main shopping street (Laugavegur) and a few blocks around it. It's colourful, quirky, and feels more like a university town than a capital. The nightlife is famously intense on weekends — Icelanders don't go out until midnight and stay out until 5am.

The whole country has a dramatic quality that's hard to overstate. Drive 30 minutes from the city and you're in a landscape that looks like another planet — lava fields, moss-covered rocks, glaciers, black sand beaches. The light in summer is extraordinary (it doesn't really get dark in June/July) and in winter the northern lights make up for the 4 hours of daylight. Iceland is expensive — possibly the most expensive country in Europe — but the landscapes are genuinely unlike anywhere else you'll go.

## Things to See

Hallgrímskirkja church dominates the Reykjavik skyline — take the lift to the top for the best views over the city and mountains. The Harpa concert hall on the harbour is architecturally stunning, all glass hexagons that catch the light differently every hour. The National Museum of Iceland is compact but excellent for understanding the Viking settlement and how this isolated island developed its own culture.

For day trips beyond the Golden Circle: the Snæfellsnes peninsula (2 hours west) has dramatic coastal scenery, the Kirkjufell mountain that's in every Instagram photo, and black pebble beaches. The south coast road takes you past Seljalandsfoss (you can walk behind the waterfall), Skógafoss, the Reynisfjara black sand beach, and the Dyrhólaey arch. In winter (October to March), northern lights tours run most clear evenings — it's genuinely magical when the sky turns green, though you need patience and luck with the weather.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Iceland is excellent. Traditional Icelandic cuisine is built around lamb, fish (especially cod and haddock), skyr (thick yoghurt), and rye bread — nuts simply aren't part of the traditional food culture. Modern restaurants in Reykjavik serve international cuisine and are very allergy-aware, with clear allergen menus and well-trained staff. Iceland follows EU-aligned allergen regulations. The only time to be cautious is with imported baked goods and chocolate, which may contain nuts, but these will be labelled clearly. "Ég er með hnetuofnæmi" is the Icelandic for nut allergy, but every restaurant in Reykjavik will understand you in English.

For vegetarians, be strategic. Reykjavik itself is manageable — Gló does excellent vegetarian and vegan bowls, Kaffi Vínyl is a vegan café and vinyl record shop (very Reykjavik), and most of the city's modern restaurants have at least one solid vegetarian main. But traditional Icelandic food is heavily animal-based: lamb, dried fish, fermented shark, whale. And once you leave Reykjavik for day trips and excursions, vegetarian options shrink dramatically. Petrol station cafés and rural restaurants often have little beyond soup (which may be lamb-based) and sandwiches. Seriously consider packing sandwiches and snacks for any day trip. The supermarkets (Bónus — look for the pig logo — is the cheapest) have decent selections for self-catering.
