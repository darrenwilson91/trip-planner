## Things to Do

The Markt square is the obvious starting point — the medieval guild houses look like they've been lifted from a picture book, and the Belfry tower in the corner is worth the 366-step climb for the view over the rooftops. On a clear day you can see all the way to the coast. From there, walk south along Wollestraat towards the Burg square, where the Basilica of the Holy Blood sits in a corner — the Romanesque lower chapel is genuinely atmospheric in a way that most churches in tourist towns aren't.

A canal boat tour is one of those things that sounds touristy but is actually the best way to see the city. The boats leave from several points along the Dijver and Rozenhoedkaai, and the perspective from the water, ducking under low stone bridges while the guide points out details you'd miss on foot, is worth the twenty minutes. Go early in the morning or late afternoon to avoid the day-tripper crush.

The beer scene is reason enough to visit. De Halve Maan brewery in the centre does tours that end with a tasting on the roof terrace — they also built an underground beer pipeline across the city, which is a good story. For something quieter, Café Rose Red on Cordoeaniersstraat has over 200 Belgian beers and the kind of low-key atmosphere where you'll end up staying longer than planned.

## The Vibe

Bruges is small enough to walk end to end in twenty minutes, which means it's also heaving with day-trippers from Brussels by mid-morning. The trick is timing — early morning and evening, when the coach parties have left, the city belongs to you. The light on the canals at dusk is genuinely beautiful, and the quiet streets around Sint-Anna and the eastern side of town feel like a different place entirely from the packed Markt.

It's unashamedly romantic and a bit chocolate-box, but that's the point. This isn't a city for edge or grit — it's for eating too many waffles, drinking strong beer in candlelit pubs, and walking along canals until your feet hurt. Two or three nights is the sweet spot. Any longer and the small-town repetition kicks in; any shorter and you'll feel rushed.

## Things to See

The Groeninge Museum on Dijver has an excellent collection of Flemish Primitives — Jan van Eyck and Hans Memling painted here, and seeing their work in the city where they lived adds something. The museum is compact enough that you won't get art fatigue. Nearby, the Gruuthuse Museum covers Bruges' history as a medieval trade powerhouse, housed in a gothic palace with a walkway bridge into the Church of Our Lady next door.

Walk east of the centre towards the Kruisvest windmills — three of the original city windmills still stand along the old ramparts, and the grassy park around them is a good spot to sit with a takeaway coffee. The Begijnhof (Beguinage) just south of the centre is a quiet courtyard of white-washed houses around a green — it closes early, so visit in the morning.

For a half-day trip, rent bikes and cycle the flat canal path to Damme, a tiny village about 7km northeast. The route follows a dead-straight canal lined with poplars, and Damme itself has a couple of good cafes and a secondhand book market.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

The elephant in the room is chocolate. Bruges has a chocolate shop roughly every fifteen metres, and almost all Belgian pralines contain nuts — hazelnuts, almonds, and pistachios are standard fillings, and even the ones that don't have whole nuts are often made on shared equipment. If you want to browse, ask specifically about nut-free options and cross-contamination. The Chocolate Line on Simon Stevinplein is one of the more creative chocolatiers and may be able to help, but don't assume anything is safe without asking.

Away from chocolate, Belgian savoury food is straightforward for nut allergies. Frites from a frituur (chip shop) are just potato and oil. Stoofvlees (Flemish beef stew with beer) is nut-free. Waffles — the plain Liège ones from street stalls — are safe, though toppings like Nutella obviously aren't. Beer is fine. The main risk is in patisseries and bakeries where marzipan and praline paste turn up in cakes and pastries.

Vegetarians will find the tourist restaurants along the Markt frustrating — most menus are built around mussels, steak, and stew. The cheese croquettes (kaaskroketten) are reliable and genuinely good. For proper vegetarian meals, try the streets south of 't Zand square where newer restaurants cater to a broader range of diets. That Bruges Waffles & Frites on Sint-Jakobsstraat does decent veggie options alongside the classics. Belgian beer and cheese culture means you won't go hungry, but expect to do a bit more menu-hunting than in Amsterdam or Berlin.
