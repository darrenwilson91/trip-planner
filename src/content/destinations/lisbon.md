## Things to Do

Spend your first morning getting lost in Alfama — it's the oldest district and the streets are so narrow and steep that Google Maps gives up trying to route you. Start at the Castelo de São Jorge at the top (the views alone are worth it) and wind your way down through tiled alleyways, past tiny fado bars and corner shops selling tinned sardines as if they were luxury goods. Which, honestly, the good ones are.

Head to LX Factory on a weekend afternoon — it's a converted industrial complex under the 25 de Abril bridge that's become a proper creative hub. Independent bookshops, design studios, restaurants, and a Sunday market. It could be insufferably trendy but somehow isn't. The rooftop bar at Rio Maravilha has sunset views across the river that justify the slightly inflated drink prices.

## The Vibe

Lisbon has a melancholy to it that's actually quite appealing — the Portuguese call it saudade, and you feel it in the fado music drifting out of Alfama bars, in the faded grandeur of the buildings, in the way people sit for hours over a single coffee. It's not a sad city, exactly, but it's a reflective one. Compare that with the energy of the Bairro Alto bar district on a Friday night and you get the full picture.

It's also properly walkable, but bring decent shoes because the hills are no joke. The tram 28 route is famous but it's essentially a tourist attraction at this point — packed and a pickpocket hotspot. Walk the same route instead, or take it early in the morning when it's quieter. The city rewards people who wander without a plan.

## Things to See

The Jerónimos Monastery in Belém is extraordinary — Manueline architecture at its most ornate, all twisted stone columns and maritime motifs. While you're out there, the Pastéis de Belém bakery next door has been making custard tarts since 1837 and the queue moves faster than it looks. MAAT (the Museum of Art, Architecture and Technology) is a short walk along the waterfront and worth visiting for the building alone.

Back in the centre, the Museu Nacional do Azulejo is essential if you want to understand why Lisbon is covered in those blue-and-white tiles — it's housed in a former convent and traces the history of Portuguese tilework from the 15th century. The Miradouro da Graça and Miradouro da Senhora do Monte give you the best panoramic views of the city, especially at sunset, and they're free.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Nut allergies are manageable in Lisbon. Portuguese savoury cooking rarely involves nuts — grilled fish, bacalhau (salt cod) dishes, caldo verde (kale soup), and rice are all safe territory. The risk area is pastries and desserts: many traditional Portuguese sweets use almonds or marzipan. Pastéis de nata (custard tarts) are fine — no nuts involved. When in doubt, "tenho alergia a nozes e amendoins" communicates the essentials. Lisbon restaurants are increasingly used to allergy requests, especially in the Chiado, Santos, and Príncipe Real areas.

For vegetarians, Lisbon has improved enormously in the last five years. Traditional Portuguese restaurants can be limiting — the classics are bacalhau, grilled sardines, and pork — but the city's newer restaurants take vegetarian food seriously. Ao 26 in Príncipe Real does a brilliant vegan tasting menu, and the food halls at Time Out Market in Cais do Sodré have multiple vegetarian-friendly stalls. The Mercado da Ribeira is also worth exploring for fresh produce. You'll eat well, but you'll need to be a bit more intentional about where you go than in, say, Barcelona.
