## Things to Do

The hawker centres are the whole point. Forget hotel restaurants — the best food in Singapore is served on plastic plates under fluorescent lights for about £3 a dish. Maxwell Food Centre is the most famous (Tian Tian chicken rice has had a Michelin Bib Gourmand since 2016), but Lau Pa Sat in the financial district and Old Airport Road Food Centre are less tourist-heavy. At Old Airport Road, the rojak stall does a fruit-and-vegetable salad with a thick peanut sauce — obviously one to avoid with a nut allergy, but it gives you an idea of how deeply peanuts run through the cuisine here.

Get up early one morning and walk through the Botanic Gardens before the heat sets in. The National Orchid Garden inside the park charges a small entry fee and has over a thousand species. It's quiet at 8am and genuinely beautiful. From there, grab the MRT to Tiong Bahru — Singapore's oldest housing estate, now its most photogenic neighbourhood, with art deco apartment blocks, independent bookshops (BooksActually), and excellent coffee at Forty Hands.

## The Vibe

Singapore is controlled, clean, and ruthlessly efficient in a way that can feel sterile for the first few hours — and then you realise how freeing it is when everything just works. The MRT runs on time to the second. The streets are safe to walk at 3am. The air conditioning in shopping malls is arctic. It's a city that was a swampy trading post sixty years ago and is now one of the richest countries on Earth, and that trajectory shows in everything from the architecture to the food.

The mix of cultures is the thing that makes it genuinely interesting. You can eat roti prata for breakfast in Little India, have dim sum in Chinatown for lunch, walk through a Malay kampong in Geylang for the afternoon, and end up at a rooftop bar on Club Street watching the container ships queue up in the strait. Kampong Glam around Arab Street has good independent shops and Haji Lane is worth a wander for its murals and tiny bars, though it gets packed on weekends.

## Things to See

Gardens by the Bay is the one thing everyone does, and it earns it. The Supertree Grove is free to walk through — go at sunset and stay for the light show at 7:45pm. The Cloud Forest dome is worth the entry fee for the waterfall alone, and the Flower Dome is the world's largest glass greenhouse, kept at a Mediterranean climate that feels bizarre when you step back into tropical humidity. Skip the OCBC Skyway between the Supertrees unless you like queuing.

Marina Bay Sands is impossible to miss — three towers with a ship balanced on top. The rooftop infinity pool is hotel guests only, but CÉ LA VI bar on level 57 is open to everyone (minimum spend, but the views are better than the paid observation deck next door). For something older, the Peranakan Museum in a former school building on Armenian Street covers the Straits Chinese culture that's unique to Singapore and Penang — the beadwork and porcelain collections are extraordinary.

Take a bumboat from Clarke Quay to Pulau Ubin, a small island off the northeast coast that feels like Singapore forty years ago. Rent a bicycle and ride through the mangroves and abandoned quarries. Chek Jawa Wetlands at the island's eastern tip has a boardwalk over the mudflats where you can spot horseshoe crabs at low tide. It's the antidote to the Marina Bay polish.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

This is a high-risk destination for nut allergies, and you need to be realistic about that. Peanuts are a foundational ingredient across multiple cuisines here. Satay sauce is ground peanuts. Gado-gado has peanut dressing. Nasi lemak often comes with fried peanuts. Many laksa pastes include candlenuts. Chinese stir-fries throw in cashews or peanuts without it being mentioned on any menu. The saving grace compared to Japan or Korea is that English is one of Singapore's four official languages — hawker stall owners, waiters, and chefs will understand when you explain your allergy.

Your safest strategy is to stick to cuisines where nut usage is more predictable. South Indian vegetarian restaurants in Little India (try Komala Vilas on Serangoon Road or Ananda Bhavan) cook primarily with lentils, rice, coconut, and spices — peanuts appear in some chutneys but the staff can tell you which. Chinese Buddhist vegetarian restaurants avoid the five pungent roots and rarely use nuts. For hawker centres, stick to dishes where you can see exactly what's going in — clear soups, grilled items, simple noodle dishes — and always ask "got peanut or not?" in the local pidgin.

For vegetarians, Singapore is surprisingly strong. The Indian food scene alone could keep you fed for a week — thosai, idli, uttapam, vegetable biryani, paneer dishes. Little India's Tekka Centre hawker stalls do excellent mee goreng and roti prata with dhal. Outside of Indian food, Thunder Tea Rice (lei cha fan) at many hawker centres is a Hakka vegetarian dish of rice with ground tea soup and vegetables. The main trap is belacan — fermented shrimp paste that appears in sambal, laksa, and many Malay dishes without being obvious. Ask specifically about shrimp paste, not just "meat."
