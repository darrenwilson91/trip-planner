## Things to Do

Walk through Gyeongbokgung Palace first thing in the morning, before the tour groups arrive. The changing of the guard ceremony happens at 10am and 2pm — it's a surprisingly impressive display with full Joseon-era military uniforms, drums, and flags. Rent a hanbok (traditional Korean clothing) from one of the dozens of shops on the streets surrounding the palace — you'll get free palace entry while wearing one, and Koreans genuinely love seeing visitors in hanbok. It's not a gimmick; families do it too.

From Gyeongbokgung, walk north to Bukchon Hanok Village. This is a residential neighbourhood of around 900 traditional hanok houses on the hills between Gyeongbokgung and Changdeokgung palaces. The narrow alleyways between the houses have views across tiled rooftops toward Namsan Tower, and it's the kind of place where you just wander. Go early or on a weekday — it's become very popular and the residents understandably get tired of crowds on their doorsteps.

For a completely different Seoul, spend an evening in Hongdae. This is the university district near Hongik University, and it's where Seoul's youth culture lives — indie music venues, street performers on the pedestrianised main drag (especially on Friday and Saturday nights), vintage clothing shops, and cheap Korean barbecue joints. It runs late. If you want something mellower, Mangwon-dong one stop north has a quieter cafe scene and the excellent Mangwon Market for street food — try the tteokbokki (spicy rice cakes) and hotteok (sweet filled pancakes).

## The Vibe

Seoul doesn't do things by halves. It's a city of 10 million people where the subway runs like clockwork, everyone's phone is newer than yours, and there's a convenience store every thirty metres. The contrast between old and new isn't subtle — you'll walk from a 14th-century palace through a gap in a wall and straight into a street of glass towers and K-pop billboards.

The food culture is intense. Eating is social and communal — Korean barbecue is cooked at the table, meals come with a small army of banchan (side dishes) that just keep appearing, and there's an unspoken expectation that you'll eat with enthusiasm. Street food markets like Gwangjang Market are chaotic and wonderful — stalls piled with kimbap, japchae, mung bean pancakes (bindaetteok), and the infamous live octopus. Myeongdong's street food stalls lean more tourist-friendly but are still decent.

Koreans are helpful if you look lost, and younger Koreans often speak reasonable English, but this is a city where having a few Korean phrases goes a long way. Download Papago (the Korean translation app by Naver — it's much better than Google Translate for Korean) and save your allergy information on your phone screen for showing to restaurant staff. T-money card from any convenience store gets you on the bus and subway — the public transport is outstanding and incredibly cheap.

## Things to See

Changdeokgung Palace and its Secret Garden (Huwon) is the better palace experience if you only have time for one — it's a UNESCO World Heritage site, less restored than Gyeongbokgung, and the garden tour through ancient woodland and pavilions is genuinely beautiful, especially in autumn. Book the garden tour in advance as numbers are limited.

Namsan Tower (N Seoul Tower) is the classic viewpoint. You can take the cable car up or walk the trail through the wooded hillside — the walk takes about 30 minutes and is surprisingly peaceful given you're in the middle of a megacity. At the top, the observation deck gives 360-degree views. Go before sunset to see the city in daylight and then lit up.

The DMZ tour is a strange, sobering experience but worth doing. Book through a reputable tour company — you can't visit independently. The JSA (Joint Security Area) at Panmunjom, where you can technically step into North Korea inside the blue conference buildings, is the headline moment, but the Dora Observatory, the Third Tunnel of Aggression, and Dorasan Station (a train station built for a reunification that hasn't happened) are equally thought-provoking.

Ikseon-dong is a hanok neighbourhood that's been converted into cafes, bars, and small shops — like Bukchon but without the residential guilt. The hanok buildings have been carefully renovated with courtyards turned into seating areas, and it's a great afternoon spot. Nearby Jogyesa Temple is the centre of Korean Zen Buddhism and occasionally hosts temple stay programmes. Insadong, the traditional craft and antiques street, connects the two — it's touristy but the tea houses down the side alleys are lovely.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Sesame is the big one in Korea — it's so fundamental to Korean cooking that many Koreans wouldn't think to mention it as an ingredient, the way a British cook might not mention salt. Sesame oil, toasted sesame seeds, and perilla (a related plant) appear in virtually every meal. If sesame is part of your nut allergy concern, Korea is particularly challenging. Ground peanuts show up in some sauces and as toppings, and pine nuts are used in traditional desserts and some porridges (juk).

Your safest bets are the department store food halls — Shinsegae Gangnam and Lotte Department Store basements have international food courts with English-speaking staff and more awareness of allergies. Korean BBQ restaurants where you grill plain meat and vegetables yourself are relatively controllable — just avoid the pre-made sauces and ask for plain salt and pepper instead. The marinade on bulgogi and galbi typically contains sesame oil, so stick with unmarinated cuts if sesame is a concern.

For vegetarians, Gwangjang Market is difficult — almost everything contains meat or seafood in some form. Temple food is your cultural and practical lifeline. Beyond Balwoo Gongyang, try Sanchon near Insadong for a temple food banquet (prix fixe, multi-course, entirely plant-based, and beautifully presented). In Itaewon, the international district, you'll find falafel, Indian restaurants, and dedicated vegetarian cafes that cater to the expat community. Plant Cafe in Itaewon and Osegyehyang (a long-running Buddhist vegetarian restaurant near Anguk station) are reliable options. Just accept that eating at most regular Korean restaurants as a vegetarian requires a lot of checking and some creative ordering — or a willingness to pick around things.
