## Things to Do

Walk the full Seawall around Stanley Park — it's about 9km and you'll pass Brockton Point totem poles, Third Beach, and Siwash Rock along the way. Hire bikes from the rental places on Denman Street and ride the whole thing in a couple of hours, or walk it in half a day. On a clear afternoon, the views of the North Shore mountains across the water are ridiculous.

Spend a morning at Granville Island Public Market. It's not a farmers' market in the usual sense — it's a permanent indoor market packed with fishmongers, bakers, specialty food stalls, and craft shops. The Lee's Donuts queue is always worth it. Wander through the artisan studios and galleries in the Net Loft building next door, then grab a beer at Granville Island Brewing.

Take the SeaBus from Waterfront Station across to North Vancouver and ride the Grouse Grind trail if you want a proper workout — it's a steep 2.9km climb locals call "Mother Nature's Stairmaster." If that sounds excessive, the Capilano Suspension Bridge park is a more relaxed option with treetop walkways and a cliff walk.

## The Vibe

Vancouver feels like a city that got lucky with its location and knows it. There's a casualness to the place — people turn up to nice restaurants in hiking gear, coffee shops double as co-working spaces, and nobody seems stressed about anything except finding parking. The pace sits somewhere between San Francisco and a ski town.

The neighbourhoods have proper character. Main Street around Mount Pleasant is where the interesting independent restaurants and shops are. Gastown has the heritage buildings and tourist pull but also some genuinely good cocktail bars (The Diamond, Pourhouse). Commercial Drive — "The Drive" — is the old Italian neighbourhood that's now a mix of indie coffee roasters, Ethiopian restaurants, and vintage shops. Kitsilano along the waterfront is more polished, with yoga studios and organic grocers living up to every West Coast stereotype.

What strikes you most is the proximity to wilderness. You're in a proper city — good transit, dense downtown, busy restaurants — but you can see snow-capped mountains from almost any street corner. It makes everything feel a bit less claustrophobic than most cities this size.

## Things to See

The Museum of Anthropology at UBC is genuinely world-class, especially the First Nations collection in Arthur Erickson's soaring glass-and-concrete building. The Bill Reid totem poles and Haida carvings are extraordinary. Budget a good couple of hours.

Queen Elizabeth Park is underrated compared to Stanley Park but worth the visit — the Bloedel Conservatory at the top has a geodesic dome full of tropical birds and plants, and the view from there takes in downtown, the mountains, and on a clear day, Mount Baker in Washington State.

If you have a spare day, drive up the Sea-to-Sky Highway to Whistler. The road itself is spectacular — following Howe Sound with mountains dropping into the ocean. Stop at Shannon Falls and the Sea to Sky Gondola in Squamish on the way. In summer, Whistler village is quieter than ski season and the mountain biking trails are world-famous.

Dr. Sun Yat-Sen Classical Chinese Garden in Chinatown is small but beautiful — the first full-scale classical Chinese garden built outside China. Vancouver's Chinatown is one of the oldest in North America, and the surrounding area (particularly the Richmond suburb south of the city) has some of the best Chinese food outside of Asia.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Canada takes food allergies seriously at a regulatory level, and Vancouver takes it even further culturally. BC restaurants are accustomed to allergy requests — it's simply part of dining culture here. You won't get eye rolls or confusion when you mention a nut allergy. Chain restaurants and packaged foods carry clear allergen labels with dedicated nut warnings.

The multicultural food scene does mean some cuisines use more nuts than others. Thai restaurants on Robson Street will use peanuts in pad thai and satay — but they're used to modifying dishes. Chinese restaurants in Richmond may use cashews and peanuts — again, just ask. Indian restaurants on Main Street and Fraser Street will use nuts in some curries and desserts. The difference from Asian destinations is that English is the first language and allergen awareness is ingrained — communication is never a barrier.

For vegetarians, Vancouver is genuinely exceptional. The Acorn on Main Street does creative seasonal vegetable dishes that would hold their own against any restaurant in the city. MeeT in Gastown and on Main Street does entirely plant-based comfort food — burgers, bowls, sharing plates — that's good enough to draw committed meat eaters. Nuba for Lebanese vegetarian mezze. Chau Veggie Express on Victoria Drive for Vietnamese-inspired plant-based food. Even the standard sushi restaurants offer impressive vegetable rolls. You'll eat brilliantly without trying.
