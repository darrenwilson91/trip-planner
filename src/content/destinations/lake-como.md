## Things to Do

Start with the ferry network — it's the whole point of Como. Buy a day pass at the landing stage in whichever town you're based and spend a full day hopping between villages. The mid-lake triangle of Bellagio, Varenna, and Menaggio is the classic route and takes a full day if you give each village a couple of hours. The slow ferry is more scenic than the hydrofoil, and the views of the mountains from the water are extraordinary in any direction.

Bellagio earns its reputation. Walk up the stepped lanes from the waterfront towards Salita Serbelloni, ducking into the tiny shops selling silk scarves (Como has been making silk for centuries). Villa Serbelloni's gardens offer guided tours with panoramic views from the promontory where the two branches of the lake split. In Varenna, take the lakeside walkway — Passeggiata degli Innamorati — that connects the old harbour to the gardens of Villa Monastero. It's short but absurdly beautiful, clinging to the cliff edge right above the water.

## The Vibe

Como operates at a pace that rewards doing very little. Mornings start with coffee on a lakeside terrace watching the early ferry cut through flat water, and the temptation to just stay there until lunch is strong. This isn't a destination for ticking off sights — it's for long meals, slow walks, and staring at mountains turning pink at sunset.

There's money here, particularly around Cernobbio and the western shore (George Clooney's villa is just south of Laglio), but it doesn't feel exclusive. The public ferries go everywhere, the best gelato costs a couple of euros, and the small villages away from Bellagio still feel like places where actual people live and hang their washing across the street. Mid-week in May or September is the sweet spot — warm enough to eat outside, quiet enough to get a lakeside table without booking three weeks in advance.

## Things to See

Villa Carlotta in Tremezzo is the standout villa — the botanical gardens alone are worth the ferry ride, especially in April and May when the azaleas and rhododendrons are flowering. The neoclassical interior has Canova sculptures and a peaceful, uncrowded feel that the bigger-name attractions can't match. Across the water in Varenna, Villa Monastero's gardens run right along the lakeshore with cypress trees framing views of the mountains.

Como town itself is the largest settlement and has a different energy — it's a working Italian city, not just a tourist village. The Duomo is impressively ornate, and the Brunate funicular takes you 700 metres up the mountainside in seven minutes for views over the entire southern basin of the lake. Walk along the lakefront promenade past the Tempio Voltiano (a museum dedicated to Alessandro Volta, who invented the electrical battery here) and the Aero Club for float plane departures. If you're up for a hike, the Greenway del Lago trail runs between Colonno and Cadenabbia — about 10km of lakeside paths through olive groves and small villages.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

The Italian nut situation applies in full around Como. Pesto alla genovese is pine nut-based and appears on most menus — ask for it to be left off or substituted. The bigger concern here is desserts: amaretti biscuits are made from ground almonds and turn up in various forms, torta della nonna has pine nuts on top, and the gelato shops stock multiple nut-based flavours alongside safe fruit options. Hazelnut is the most common nut in gelato — stracciatella, fior di latte, and fruit sorbets are your safe choices.

For savoury courses, you're on solid ground. Risotto al porcini, pasta with local cheese, polenta taragna (buckwheat polenta with melted cheese), and bruschetta are all naturally nut-free. Lake fish like missoltini (dried shad) and lavarello (whitefish) are simple preparations — grilled or baked without nut-based coatings. The trick is sauces: any pesto, any walnut sauce (salsa di noci), and some agrodolce preparations can contain nuts. Ask "contiene frutta a guscio?" at every restaurant, every time.

Vegetarians will find plenty to eat. Risotto is the star — mushroom, asparagus, and saffron versions are standard. Fresh pasta with butter and sage (burro e salvia) is a northern Italian classic and appears on almost every menu. Polenta served with local cheeses and grilled vegetables is a hearty mountain option. The main limitation is in the smallest villages where a fixed menu might have one primi and one secondi, both featuring meat or fish — in those cases, ask if the kitchen can do a simple pasta or risotto, and they almost always will.
