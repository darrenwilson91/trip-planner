## Things to Do

Trastevere in the evening is non-negotiable — cross the Tiber as the light goes golden and wander the cobblestoned streets looking for somewhere with no English menu and a couple of nonnas eating at the bar. Da Enzo al 29 on Via dei Vascellari does cacio e pepe that'll ruin the dish for you anywhere else. Get there early (by Roman standards, that means 7:30pm) because the queue gets long.

The Vatican Museums need a full morning — book the first slot and be at the door when it opens. The Sistine Chapel is at the very end of the route, and if you get there early enough, you might have thirty seconds of relative calm before the crowds arrive. The Colosseum and Forum together take another half day; get the combined ticket and do the Forum first because it closes earlier. For a break from antiquity, the Aventine Hill keyhole (Piazza dei Cavalieri di Malta) frames St Peter's dome perfectly through a garden — free and almost never a long wait.

## The Vibe

Rome operates on its own timeline and you either accept that or have a frustrating trip. Lunch is at 1pm, dinner at 9pm, and if you try to eat at 6pm you'll find locked doors. This is a city where people argue about whether the pizza bianca from Roscioli or Bonci is superior, where a bad espresso is a personal affront, and where a bus might arrive on time or might not arrive at all.

The centro storico around Piazza Navona and Campo de' Fiori is beautiful but heavily touristed. Cross the river to Trastevere for a more local feel. Testaccio is the old slaughterhouse district and has some of the best traditional Roman food — it's where the dishes you now see on every menu were invented. Monti, near the Colosseum, has the best independent shopping and aperitivo bars.

## Things to See

Beyond the obvious trinity of Colosseum, Forum, and Vatican — the Pantheon is free and still stops you in your tracks. Stand underneath the oculus when it's raining for the full effect. The Borghese Gallery requires advance booking (they limit numbers strictly) but the Bernini sculptures inside are worth any logistical hassle. Apollo and Daphne alone is one of the greatest things anyone has ever carved from stone.

The Appian Way on a Sunday, when it's closed to traffic, is an extraordinary walk — ancient Roman road stretching out into the countryside past tombs and catacombs. Rent bikes from the visitor centre and cycle it. The Capitoline Museums on the Campidoglio are the world's oldest public museums and consistently less crowded than the Vatican. For modern art, MAXXI in the Flaminio district is worth the trek — Zaha Hadid's building is as much the attraction as the exhibitions inside.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Rome requires more vigilance than northern European cities. The specific risks: pesto (pine nuts — this appears in pasta, on bruschetta, sometimes in salads), hazelnut in many desserts (tiramisù sometimes, torta della nonna, gelato), and amaretti biscuits (almonds). Pasta with simple sauces (cacio e pepe, amatriciana, carbonara) is safe. Pizza is safe. Supplì (fried rice balls) are safe. Always say "ho un'allergia alla frutta a guscio" and specifically ask "contiene noci, nocciole, mandorle, o pinoli?" — the specific nut names help because "frutta a guscio" is a broad category and waiters may not think of pine nuts as nuts. Gelaterias: check every flavour, as cross-contamination with hazelnut (nocciola) is common.

For vegetarians, Rome is a pleasure. Cacio e pepe (pasta with pecorino and black pepper) is the city's signature dish and it's meat-free. So are margherita pizza, suppli, fried artichokes (carciofi alla giudia — a Jewish-Roman speciality from the Ghetto), and most antipasti spreads. Vegetable contorni (side dishes) are substantial enough to make a meal — puntarelle with anchovy dressing is everywhere in winter (skip the anchovy version if you're strict veggie). The main thing to watch is that some pasta shapes come with meat ragù by default — amatriciana and gricia traditionally use guanciale (pork cheek) — so just check when ordering.
