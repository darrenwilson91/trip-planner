## Things to Do

Walk through San Telmo on a Sunday when the entire neighbourhood becomes a street market. Calle Defensa fills with antique stalls, leather goods, and mate sets, while tango dancers perform on the cobblestones at Plaza Dorrego. Get there by mid-morning before it gets too packed, and work your way south towards Parque Lezama where the crowds thin out and the jacarandas bloom purple in October.

Book a tango show if you want the full spectacle — Café de los Angelitos or El Viejo Almacén put on slick productions — but for something more authentic, find a milonga. La Catedral in Almagro is a low-key space in a converted warehouse where locals actually dance. You don't need to participate; watching from the bar with a glass of Malbec is a perfectly good evening.

Take a day trip to Tigre, an hour north by train from Retiro station. The Paraná Delta is a maze of waterways lined with wooden houses on stilts, rowing clubs, and cafés you can only reach by boat. Rent a kayak or take the public lanchas (water buses) between islands. It feels nothing like the city you just left.

## The Vibe

Buenos Aires operates on its own clock. Dinner starts at 10pm, bars fill up after midnight, and Sunday is for long lunches that blur into evening. The porteño attitude is theatrical and warm — people will talk to you for an hour about the best place to get medialunas (small croissants) and genuinely mean every word.

The city looks like Paris and sounds like Naples, which makes sense given the immigration waves that built it. Recoleta has Haussmann-style apartment blocks and manicured parks. San Telmo has crumbling facades with tango music drifting from upstairs windows. Palermo has tree-lined streets with independent boutiques and sidewalk cafés. Each barrio has its own rhythm and the best way to find it is to walk — this is a supremely walkable city once you get past the initial scale of the place.

The economic situation means Buenos Aires is remarkably affordable for what you get. A proper sit-down dinner with wine rarely tops £15. A café con leche and three medialunas is about £2. Hotels that would cost three times as much in any European capital are genuinely reasonable here.

## Things to See

Recoleta Cemetery is not a normal cemetery — it's a city of marble mausoleums where Argentina's elite are buried in ornate family vaults. Eva Perón's tomb is here, marked by flowers from visitors. The scale and craftsmanship is extraordinary. Afterwards, walk through the weekend craft market outside and grab lunch on the terrace at La Biela, a grand café that's been open since 1850.

The Teatro Colón is one of the world's great opera houses. Even if you don't catch a performance, the guided tour is worth it — the acoustics, the seven-storey stage machinery, and the stained glass dome are staggering. Book the English-language tour online to avoid the queue.

La Boca is the famous neighbourhood with Caminito's painted tin houses in primary colours, originally immigrant dockworkers' homes. It's touristy along Caminito itself, but the street art and the chaos of matchday at La Bombonera (Boca Juniors' stadium) give it genuine energy. Don't wander too far from the main streets here though — the surrounding area is rough.

MALBA (Museo de Arte Latinoamericano de Buenos Aires) in Palermo has an excellent collection of Latin American modern art — Frida Kahlo, Diego Rivera, Antonio Berni. The building itself is sharp modernist architecture. Better than the larger national museums for a focused visit.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Buenos Aires is one of the safer long-haul destinations. The Argentine kitchen runs on beef, chimichurri (parsley, garlic, vinegar, oil — no nuts), and simple grilled preparations. Empanadas are stuffed with meat, cheese, or corn — nuts don't feature. The main area of caution is heladerías (ice cream shops), which are everywhere and excellent. Flavours with dulce de leche, fruit, or chocolate are safe, but ask about praline, pistachio, and any "crocante" flavours which use chopped nuts. Alfajores (the ubiquitous chocolate-coated sandwich cookies) are sometimes made with ground almonds, but the mass-produced ones like Havanna are nut-free — check artisanal versions.

For vegetarians, be honest with yourself about expectations. A traditional parrilla will have one vegetarian option if you're lucky — provoleta (grilled provolone with oregano) and perhaps a mixed salad. Even the salads sometimes come with ham. The phrase "soy vegetariano/a, no como carne ni pollo ni pescado" (I'm vegetarian, I don't eat meat, chicken, or fish) is essential because "vegetariano" alone sometimes gets interpreted as "no red meat."

Your best bet is Palermo Soho, where restaurants like Buenos Aires Verde and Artemisia do proper plant-based menus. Pizza is everywhere and reliably good — try a fugazzeta (thick crust, loads of onions and cheese) or fainá (chickpea flatbread often served alongside pizza). Bakeries always have tartas de verdura (vegetable pies) and empanadas de humita that make solid cheap lunches. Breakfast is easy — medialunas, tostadas, and good coffee are standard everywhere.
