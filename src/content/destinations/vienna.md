## Things to Do

Start at the Naschmarkt on a Saturday morning when the flea market extends the regular food stalls. Grab a plate of falafel or stuffed vine leaves from one of the Middle Eastern stands, then work your way through the cheese and olive vendors. The market runs along the Wienzeile from Karlsplatz to Kettenbrückengasse and it's the best place to get a feel for what Viennese people actually eat day-to-day, away from the tourist schnitzel spots.

Book tickets for the Kunsthistorisches Museum and give it at least three hours — the Bruegel room alone is worth the trip. If you're into contemporary art, the MuseumsQuartier across the road has the Leopold Museum (Schiele and Klimt) and MUMOK. On a warm evening, the courtyard of the MuseumsQuartier fills up with locals sitting on the colourful benches, and it's a great spot to people-watch with a glass of Grüner Veltliner.

## The Vibe

Vienna feels like a city that takes itself seriously but doesn't mind if you don't. There's a formality to things — the coffee houses have waiters in waistcoats, the opera house demands smart dress — but then you'll stumble on a wine tavern in a courtyard where everyone's drinking Sturm (half-fermented grape juice) from plastic cups. The 7th district around Neubaugasse has the most relaxed energy: independent shops, good cafés, and a younger crowd.

The coffee house culture is genuinely special and not just a tourist thing. Café Central is the famous one (Trotsky used to play chess there), but it's rammed. Try Café Sperl on Gumpendorfer Straße instead — same vibe, fewer selfie sticks. Order a Melange (Vienna's answer to a cappuccino) and a newspaper, and sit for as long as you like. Nobody will rush you.

## Things to See

Schönbrunn Palace is the big one — the audio tour of the state rooms takes about an hour, but the gardens are free and stretch up a hill to the Gloriette with views back over the whole city. Go early or late to avoid the coach-tour crowds. The Belvedere is slightly less overwhelming and has Klimt's "The Kiss" — smaller palace, beautiful gardens, and usually quieter.

Walk along the Ringstrasse, the grand boulevard that circles the old city. You'll pass the Parliament, the Rathaus (town hall), the Burgtheater, and the University — all absurdly grand buildings from the same period. Duck into Stephansdom cathedral in the centre — climb the south tower for the best rooftop view in Vienna, 343 steps with no lift.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

The nut situation in Vienna is genuinely tricky because the cake and pastry culture is so central to the experience. You'll want to visit a coffee house, and the cake display will be full of things you can't eat — Sachertorte (the famous chocolate cake) is one of the few safe options as it's traditionally nut-free, though check with the waiter. Apple strudel is usually safe but confirm it wasn't made alongside nut strudel. Avoid anything with "Nuss" (nut), "Mandel" (almond), or "Marzipan" in the name.

For savoury eating, you're in much better shape. Wiener Schnitzel (the real veal version or the chicken alternative) is breadcrumbed and fried, no nuts involved. Potato soup, goulash, Käsekrainer (cheese sausage), and bread dumplings are all safe. At the Naschmarkt, the Middle Eastern and Asian food stalls give you plenty of nut-free options. Modern restaurants in the Neubau district are allergy-aware and will have English-speaking staff who understand dietary requirements.

For vegetarians, the traditional Beisl (pub-restaurants) will usually have Käsespätzle, Eierschwammerl (chanterelle mushroom dishes in season), and various dumpling options. The dedicated vegetarian spots like Tian (which has a Michelin star) and Swing Kitchen (vegan fast food chain) are excellent. Around the university area in the 9th district, you'll find affordable veggie lunch spots catering to students.
