## Things to Do

Skip the Champs-Élysées and spend your walking time in the neighbourhoods instead. Le Marais (3rd and 4th arrondissements) is where you want to be — medieval streets, the best falafel in Europe at L'As du Fallafel on Rue des Rosiers, independent galleries, and the Place des Vosges, which is the most beautiful square in Paris and somehow not overrun with tourists. On Sundays, the Rue des Francs-Bourgeois closes to traffic and the whole area becomes wonderfully walkable.

The Canal Saint-Martin in the 10th is the Paris that Parisians actually live in — iron footbridges, café terraces along the water, and a string of excellent restaurants and wine bars. Walk from République north along the canal to Parc des Buttes-Chaumont, which has cliffs, a waterfall, a temple on a hilltop, and is inexplicably unknown to most visitors. For markets, the Marché d'Aligre near Bastille is the best in the city — cheaper than touristy spots and with an incredible covered hall.

## The Vibe

Paris has a reputation for being difficult and that reputation is about 20 years out of date. It's genuinely one of the most walkable cities in Europe, the restaurant scene has loosened up from its starched-tablecloth traditions, and the natural wine bar culture in the 10th, 11th, and 20th arrondissements is some of the best drinking in the world. You still need to learn a few phrases of French — "bonjour" when entering any shop is non-negotiable, and attempting "excusez-moi" before asking something in English goes a long way.

The city is arranged in arrondissements that spiral out from the centre and each has its own character. The 6th (Saint-Germain) is old-money intellectual. The 11th (Oberkampf, Bastille) is young and restaurant-dense. Montmartre (18th) is touristy at the Sacré-Cœur but brilliantly local one street behind. Belleville (20th) is the most diverse neighbourhood and has excellent Chinese and North African food.

## Things to See

The Musée d'Orsay is a better museum experience than the Louvre — it's manageable in half a day, the Impressionist collection is unmatched, and the building (a converted railway station) is beautiful. If you do the Louvre, accept that you can't see everything and pick three things you want to find. The Winged Victory and Vermeer's Lacemaker are often less crowded than the Mona Lisa.

The Fondation Louis Vuitton in the Bois de Boulogne is architecturally spectacular — Frank Gehry's glass sails are worth the trip even if you're not interested in the exhibitions. The Musée Rodin has a sculpture garden that makes for a perfect afternoon with a coffee from the garden café. The Sainte-Chapelle on the Île de la Cité has the most extraordinary stained glass — 1,113 panels from the 13th century that turn the interior into a kaleidoscope on sunny days. It's small, so the queues move quickly.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Paris demands vigilance — specifically around bakeries and patisseries. French baking culture is built on almonds (frangipane, financiers, macarons), hazelnuts (praline, Paris-Brest), pistachios (various pastries), and walnuts (in salads and some cheeses like Roquefort aux noix). If you walk into a boulangerie, assume most things behind the counter have touched nuts. Savoury food is much safer: steak frites, onion soup, croque monsieur, omelettes, ratatouille, and most bistro classics are nut-free. The key phrases: "J'ai une allergie aux noix" (I have a nut allergy), and be specific — "noix" (walnuts), "noisettes" (hazelnuts), "amandes" (almonds), "cacahuètes" (peanuts), "pignons" (pine nuts). French allergen law requires restaurants to provide allergen information, but enforcement is less consistent than in the UK. Ask clearly, and don't be shy about repeating yourself.

For vegetarians, Paris has turned a corner. Ten years ago it was a struggle — now there are excellent dedicated vegetarian restaurants: Le Potager de Charlotte in the 10th, Café Ginger in the 4th, Wild & The Moon for quick plant-based lunches. The falafel shops in Le Marais are reliably excellent (L'As du Fallafel, Mi-Va-Mi). Cheese shops and fromageries are obviously a paradise for vegetarians who eat dairy — a baguette, some Comté, and a bench in the Jardin du Luxembourg is one of the best meals in Paris. The main trap: many French soups and sauces use chicken or veal stock as a base. "C'est végétarien?" doesn't always account for stock, so ask "c'est fait avec du bouillon de viande?" if you want to be sure.
