## Things to Do

Start at Jemaa el-Fnaa in the early evening, not midday. During the day the square is half-empty and unpleasantly hot — it comes alive after 5pm when the food stalls set up, the smoke rises, and the musicians, storytellers, and henna artists appear. Grab a fresh orange juice from one of the stalls (they're all roughly the same, don't overthink it) and just walk through the chaos for a while before committing to a food stall.

The souks radiate north from the square in a warren that's genuinely disorienting on your first visit. The trick is to not worry about getting lost — you will, and that's half the point. The dyers' souk (Souk des Teinturiers) is worth seeking out for the skeins of coloured wool hung across the alley to dry. The metalworkers around Place Seffarine are another highlight — you'll hear them before you see them. If you want to buy anything, the starting price is typically three to four times what they'll accept, so negotiate cheerfully and walk away if the price doesn't feel right. Nobody takes it personally.

Book a traditional hammam experience — La Mamounia's spa is the luxury option, but Hammam Dar el-Bacha in the medina is more authentic and a fraction of the price. You'll be scrubbed with black soap and a kessa glove until you feel like a new person.

## The Vibe

Marrakech hits you with everything at once — the call to prayer echoing off terracotta walls at dawn, the smell of cedarwood and cumin in the souks, motorbikes weaving through pedestrians in alleys barely wide enough for two people. It's intense, and it's not for everyone. If you need things to be predictable and orderly, this isn't your city. But if you're up for surrendering to the chaos, it rewards you constantly.

The riad experience is essential. These are traditional courtyard houses converted into guesthouses, and staying in one — with its tiled floors, central fountain, and rooftop terrace — is completely different from a hotel. Riad Yasmine in the medina has one of the most photographed plunge pools in Morocco, but there are dozens of beautiful options. Book one inside the medina walls so you can walk to everything. The contrast between the plain exterior doors and the ornate interiors is one of the great pleasures of the city.

Evenings on a rooftop terrace with mint tea and a view of the Koutoubia Mosque minaret is the way to end each day. The sunsets over the medina rooftops, with the Atlas Mountains going pink in the distance, are genuinely special.

## Things to See

The Jardin Majorelle is the headline attraction and worth it despite the crowds — Yves Saint Laurent bought the property in 1980 and the cobalt blue buildings against the cacti and bougainvillea are extraordinary. Go when it opens to avoid the worst of the queues. The adjacent Musée Yves Saint Laurent Marrakech is a beautiful piece of architecture by Studio KO, even if fashion isn't your thing.

The Bahia Palace is a 19th-century masterpiece of zellige tilework, carved cedarwood, and painted ceilings. It's less crowded than the Jardin Majorelle and arguably more impressive — the Grand Courtyard with its marble floor is one of the most beautiful spaces in the city. The Saadian Tombs nearby were walled up for centuries and only rediscovered in 1917; the Chamber of Twelve Columns is extraordinary craftsmanship.

Day trip to the Atlas Mountains — the Ourika Valley is about ninety minutes' drive and a completely different world. The Berber villages clinging to the hillsides, the Setti Fatma waterfalls, and the terraced agriculture along the valley floor make for a brilliant day out. You can arrange a driver through your riad for a reasonable price, typically around 500-700 MAD (£40-55) for the day. If you have more time, the drive over the Tizi n'Tichka pass to Aït Benhaddou — the fortified ksar that's appeared in every desert film you've ever seen — is spectacular, though it's a long day.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

**This is the most challenging destination in this entire guide for nut allergies.** Almonds are foundational to Moroccan cooking in a way that goes far beyond desserts. Pastilla (the signature pastry) layers pigeon or chicken with ground almonds, sugar, and cinnamon. Many tagines are finished with toasted almonds or contain ground almond paste in the sauce. Couscous is frequently garnished with almonds and raisins. Amlou — a dip made from argan oil, almonds, and honey — is served at breakfast in almost every riad. Moroccan salads can contain walnuts. Desserts like chebakia are fried, dipped in honey, and rolled in sesame and ground almonds.

The difficulty is that nuts are often ground or blended into dishes so you cannot see them. Communicating the allergy is harder here than in Western Europe — French is widely spoken in tourist areas, so learning "je suis allergique aux noix et aux cacahuètes" (I am allergic to nuts and peanuts) is essential. A printed card in both French and Arabic is strongly recommended. Even so, understanding of the severity of nut allergies is lower than in the UK, so be prepared to explain that this means you cannot eat dishes that have been cooked with nuts, not just dishes with visible nuts on top.

The safest options are simple grilled meats (brochettes), plain couscous with vegetables (specify no almonds), bread with olive oil, harira soup (lentil and tomato based, generally nut-free), and salads you've confirmed are nut-free. Avoid pastries entirely — the entire Moroccan patisserie tradition is built on almonds and honey.

For vegetarians, Morocco is workable. Vegetable tagines are on every menu and are genuinely good — the slow-cooked method concentrates flavour beautifully. Zaalouk (smoky aubergine and tomato dip) and taktouka (cooked pepper and tomato salad) are excellent starters. Bessara (broad bean soup) is a Marrakech street food staple, especially for breakfast. Harira soup is traditionally vegetarian during Ramadan. The main challenge is that meat stock can appear in dishes described as vegetable-based, so ask specifically. Riads with set menus will usually accommodate vegetarians if you tell them at breakfast what you'd like for dinner.
