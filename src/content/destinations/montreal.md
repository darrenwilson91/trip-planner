## Things to Do

Walk through the Plateau Mont-Royal on a weekday morning when the neighbourhood is at its most relaxed. The colourful spiral staircases on every building front, the tiny front gardens, the corner dépanneurs — it's the Montreal you've seen in photos but it feels genuinely lived-in rather than staged. Head up Avenue Fairmount to grab a bagel from Fairmount Bagel (open 24 hours, wood-fired, nothing like a London bagel) and eat it still warm on the pavement outside. The eternal debate is Fairmount versus St-Viateur a few blocks south — you should try both and pick a side.

Rent bikes from BIXI (Montreal's bike-share, stations everywhere) and ride up to the top of Mont Royal. The Kondiaronk Belvedere lookout gives you a full panorama of the downtown skyline, the St. Lawrence River, and on a clear day, across to the Monteregian Hills. The park itself was designed by Frederick Law Olmsted (same landscape architect as Central Park) and is the green heart of the city. In autumn, the colours up here are extraordinary — if you can time your trip for late September or early October, do it.

Spend an afternoon at Jean-Talon Market in Little Italy. It's not a tourist market — it's where Montrealers actually shop. The produce stands are incredible in summer (Quebec strawberries, heirloom tomatoes, fresh herbs), and there are specialty shops around the edges for cheese, olive oil, spices, and prepared foods. Grab lunch from the empanada stand or the Lebanese counter at the back.

## The Vibe

Montreal feels like someone took the best parts of a French city — the food culture, the café terraces, the general attitude that life should be enjoyed — and dropped them into a North American grid with better winters gear and cheaper rent. The city is bilingual in a way that's genuinely seamless; most people switch between French and English mid-conversation without thinking about it. You'll get by fine in English everywhere, but even a few words of French are appreciated.

The neighbourhoods have proper identities. Mile End is creative and slightly scruffy — record shops, independent bookstores, the bagel places, and some of the city's best restaurants operating out of tiny storefronts. The Plateau is residential and beautiful, all those famous staircases and tree-lined streets. Old Montreal along the waterfront has the cobblestones and the 17th-century stone buildings — it's touristy but genuinely worth seeing, especially the Basilique Notre-Dame (the interior is astonishing, all deep blue and gold). The Mile-Ex area just north of Mile End is where the newest restaurants and galleries are opening up.

There's a creative energy here that you notice quickly. The street art is everywhere and it's good — massive murals covering entire building sides, not just tags. In June, the city essentially becomes one long festival — jazz, comedy (Just for Laughs), electronic music (Mutek), Francofolies for French-language music. Even outside festival season, there's always something happening.

## Things to See

The Basilique Notre-Dame in Old Montreal is the single most impressive interior in the city. It was completed in 1829 and the neo-Gothic woodwork inside, all painted in deep blues, reds, and gold leaf, is genuinely jaw-dropping. They run an evening light show called Aura that's worth seeing if you don't mind the tourist price. The rest of Old Montreal rewards an hour or two of wandering — Place Jacques-Cartier, the old port warehouses now converted to galleries and restaurants, and the views along Rue de la Commune.

The Montreal Museum of Fine Arts on Rue Sherbrooke is world-class and partially free. The permanent collection spans from Old Masters to contemporary Québécois art, and the temporary exhibitions are always ambitious. The museum campus spans both sides of the street with an underground tunnel connecting the buildings. A few blocks east, the Musée d'Art Contemporain has reopened after renovation and focuses on challenging contemporary work.

Take the metro to Parc Jean-Drapeau on the island in the St. Lawrence River. The Biosphère (Buckminster Fuller's geodesic dome from Expo 67) is an architecture landmark worth seeing even from outside. The park itself is good for a walk or a swim in summer. From here you can see the Jacques Cartier Bridge — walk back across it at sunset for one of the best views of the city skyline.

The Quartier des Spectacles downtown comes alive at night with permanent light installations. Even when there's no festival on, the illuminated fountains and projection art on building facades make it worth a post-dinner walk.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Canada's allergen legislation is among the strongest globally, and Montreal's restaurant culture takes it seriously. Menus frequently list major allergens, and staff understand cross-contamination. You can eat at most restaurants with confidence after a straightforward conversation with your server — no awkward negotiations or blank looks.

The main area of caution is French-influenced patisseries. Montreal has excellent bakeries and many use almond flour (frangipane, financiers, macarons), hazelnut (praline), and walnut in their pastries. But these are clearly distinct items and the staff will know exactly what's in everything. Savoury food across the board — whether it's a smoked meat sandwich at Schwartz's, poutine from La Banquise, or a bowl at a Vietnamese restaurant on Rue Saint-Denis — is reliably nut-free. Asian restaurants on Boulevard Saint-Laurent sometimes use peanuts or cashews, but English is universal here so communicating is straightforward.

For vegetarians, the modern food scene is excellent. Aux Vivres in Mile End has been doing creative plant-based food since before it was trendy. LOV has multiple locations with a polished vegetarian menu. The Plateau and Mile End are full of restaurants where the vegetarian options aren't an afterthought. Poutine — the quintessential Montreal dish — is vegetarian in its classic form (fries, cheese curds, gravy), though some places use beef-based gravy so it's worth asking. The bagels are a highlight: Fairmount and St-Viateur are both vegetarian, wood-fired, and utterly different from anything you'll find at home. At Jean-Talon Market, you can assemble an incredible vegetarian meal from the cheese, bread, and produce vendors.
