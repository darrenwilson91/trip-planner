## Things to Do

Rent a bike on your first morning — it's the only way to travel here that makes any sense. The city is dead flat, the bike lanes are separated and well-marked, and you'll cover three times more ground than on foot. Cycle through the Jordaan, which is all independent boutiques and brown cafés (traditional Dutch pubs, nothing to do with the colour), then out to the Vondelpark for a sit-down when your legs need a break.

The Albert Cuyp Market in De Pijp runs every day except Sunday and it's the real deal — not a tourist market but where locals actually shop. Stroopwafels made fresh, Dutch cheese, flowers, fabric. Grab lunch from one of the Surinamese stalls (roti is usually nut-free but ask about peanut). If you want the museum experience, the Rijksmuseum deserves a full morning — the Vermeer rooms alone justify the visit.

## The Vibe

Amsterdam manages to be simultaneously laid-back and culturally intense. People sit along the canals drinking beer at 3pm on a Tuesday, but there are also three Michelin-starred restaurants and one of the best contemporary art scenes in Europe. The Jordaan feels like a village within a city, the Negen Straatjes (Nine Streets) are brilliant for independent shopping, and De Pijp has the energy of a neighbourhood that's been gentrifying for a decade but hasn't lost its personality yet.

It's small enough that you can walk everywhere, though cycling is genuinely the move. The canal ring is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and once you've walked it at night, with the bridge lights reflecting off the water, you'll understand why. It's not just pretty — it's properly atmospheric.

## Things to See

The Rijksmuseum is the obvious headliner and deserves the hype — Rembrandt's Night Watch, Vermeer's Milkmaid, and a building that's a work of art itself. The Van Gogh Museum next door is excellent but gets crushingly crowded; book a late-afternoon slot. For contemporary art, the Stedelijk Museum completes the Museumplein trio and is often overlooked.

The Anne Frank House is essential but you need to book tickets exactly 6 weeks in advance — they sell out within minutes. If you miss them, the nearby Westerkerk is worth visiting regardless. For something unexpected, the Houseboat Museum on the Prinsengracht gives you a look inside an actual canal houseboat, and the Foam photography museum is consistently excellent. On a sunny day, the NDSM Wharf in Amsterdam-Noord (free ferry from Centraal) is a converted shipyard with street art, craft breweries, and a completely different energy to the centre.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Amsterdam is a comfortable city. Traditional Dutch food — stamppot (mashed potato with veg), bitterballen (fried beef croquettes), poffertjes (mini pancakes), herring — is nut-free. The Dutch are generally very allergy-aware, and restaurants are legally required to provide allergen information. The main watch-outs are Indonesian restaurants (peanut sauce — satay and gado gado — is everywhere, and peanuts can appear in curries and stir-fries) and some Dutch bakeries that use almonds in pastries like gevulde koeken. "Ik heb een notenallergie" will be understood, though most restaurant staff speak fluent English anyway.

For vegetarians, Amsterdam is outstanding. The city has embraced plant-based eating with genuine enthusiasm. Meatless District does entirely vegan comfort food, De Bolhoed has been doing vegetarian dining since before it was fashionable, and Terrain in De Pijp does a brilliant veggie tasting menu. Indonesian rijsttafel at restaurants like Kantjil & de Tijger offers loads of vegetarian dishes. Even traditional brown cafés now have decent veggie options on their bar food menus. The Albert Cuyp Market and Foodhallen both have multiple plant-based stalls. This is one of those cities where being vegetarian is genuinely an advantage — you'll discover restaurants you'd miss otherwise.
