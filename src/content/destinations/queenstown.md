## Things to Do

Queenstown exists because of what surrounds it — the Southern Alps, Lake Wakatipu, and enough outdoor activities to fill a month. The obvious starting point is the Shotover Jet, a jet boat that hammers through narrow canyon walls at ridiculous speed. It's genuinely thrilling even if you're not usually an adrenaline person. The original AJ Hackett bungee at Kawarau Bridge is where commercial bungee jumping was invented in 1988, and jumping off that bridge with the turquoise river below you is one of those things that stays with you.

For something less heart-stopping, take the Skyline Gondola up Bob's Peak. The view from the top — lake, mountains, town laid out below — is worth the ticket alone, but you can also do luge runs back down, which are surprisingly good fun. In winter (June to August), the Remarkables and Coronet Peak ski fields are within thirty minutes of town. Neither is enormous by European standards, but the scenery makes up for it and lift queues are short by comparison.

The Routeburn Track is one of New Zealand's Great Walks and starts about an hour from Queenstown. You don't need to do the full multi-day tramp — the first section to Key Summit is a manageable day walk with views across the Darran Mountains and Hollyford Valley that genuinely make you stop and stare. Closer to town, the walk around Queenstown Hill (Te Tapu-nui) takes about three hours and gives panoramic views without needing a car.

## The Vibe

Queenstown has the energy of a ski resort crossed with a backpacker hub. It's small — the town centre takes about fifteen minutes to walk end to end — and it runs on tourism, which means it's friendly and well-organised but not particularly authentic. The waterfront along Marine Parade is lovely in the evening, with the Remarkables turning pink at sunset across the lake. Earnslaw Park is where locals and visitors sit on the grass with fish and chips watching the TSS Earnslaw, a 1912 coal-fired steamship, putter across the lake.

The eating-out scene punches above what you'd expect from a town this size. Fergburger is the institution — there'll be a queue at midnight and it's earned. Rata, by local chef Josh Emett, does proper fine dining with New Zealand produce. For drinks, Atlas Beer Cafe has a solid craft beer selection and The Winery does wine flights if you want to try Central Otago Pinot Noir, which is genuinely world-class from this region. The newer laneway developments around Searle Lane have brought in better casual dining beyond the burger-and-pizza default.

## Things to See

Milford Sound is the big day trip and it lives up to its reputation. The drive alone — through the Eglinton Valley, past Mirror Lakes, through the Homer Tunnel — takes about four hours each way and is spectacular throughout. The cruise on the fiord itself, with Mitre Peak rising straight up out of the water and waterfalls pouring off the cliff faces, is the kind of scenery that makes you put your phone down and just stare. Book a smaller boat operator rather than the large cruise ships if you can — less crowded and they get closer to the falls.

Arrowtown is twenty minutes from Queenstown and worth a half-day. It's a gold-rush era settlement with a preserved main street of stone cottages, good cafes, and the eerie remains of the Chinese miners' settlement by the Arrow River. In autumn (April-May), the trees along the river turn extraordinary shades of gold and red. Glenorchy, at the head of Lake Wakatipu, is a forty-five minute drive on a road that follows the lake shore with mountains on both sides — it was used as a Lord of the Rings filming location (Isengard, Lothlórien) and honestly, you'll see why immediately.

The Gibbston Valley wine region is a short drive toward Cromwell and specialises in Pinot Noir that competes with Burgundy at a fraction of the price. Chard Farm, perched improbably on a cliff above the Kawarau Gorge, is the most dramatic winery setting you'll ever visit. Peregrine has good tastings and a striking architecture. If you're there in summer, the drive over the Crown Range to Wanaka — another lake town, quieter than Queenstown — is one of New Zealand's best scenic roads.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

New Zealand's food safety regulations require clear allergen disclosure, and in practice this works well. Restaurants take allergies seriously, menus often mark common allergens, and staff will check with the kitchen without making it awkward. Supermarkets (New World, Countdown, Pak'nSave) have excellent allergen labelling on packaged food. For a nut allergy, you can eat out in Queenstown with a high degree of confidence — this is one of the easiest destinations on our list.

The vegetarian situation is more limited, but manageable. Queenstown's food scene is weighted toward adventure-tourism fuel — big burgers, lamb shanks, steak. Most restaurants will have one or two vegetarian mains, but they're often afterthoughts. The better options are at the newer restaurants: Rata does a good vegetarian tasting menu if you ask, Bespoke Kitchen on Searle Lane is strong for brunch with plenty of plant-based choices, and the Thai and Indian restaurants in town (Tanoshi, Little India) have reliable vegetarian sections. Fergburger's "Bun Laden" is the go-to veggie burger and it's genuinely good. Self-catering from the Raeward Fresh or New World supermarkets gives you more control. If you're heading to Wanaka for a day, Fedeli has better vegetarian options than most of Queenstown.
