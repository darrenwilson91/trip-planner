## Things to Do

Walk the Royal Mile end to end — start at the Castle and finish at Holyrood Palace, but the real pleasure is ducking into the closes (narrow alleyways) that branch off on either side. Advocate's Close and Riddle's Court are particularly atmospheric. Half-way down, Victoria Street curves downhill in a sweep of colourful shopfronts — it's genuinely photogenic and the shops are actually interesting (not just tartan tat).

Arthur's Seat is a proper hill walk in the middle of the city — an extinct volcano that takes about 45 minutes to climb and rewards you with 360-degree views of Edinburgh, the Firth of Forth, and on clear days, the Highlands. Start from the Holyrood end and take the path that curves around the back — it's a gentler gradient than the direct route. Dean Village, tucked into the Water of Leith valley, is a 15-minute walk from the West End and feels like you've left the city entirely — old mill buildings, a river walk, and almost no tourists.

## The Vibe

Edinburgh is a city of contrasts that actually works. The medieval Old Town with its tenements and wynds sits across the valley from the Georgian New Town with its orderly crescents and grand terraces. The Castle looms over everything. In August, the Festival transforms the entire city into a stage — comedy, theatre, music spilling out of every venue, basement, and car park. It's chaotic and brilliant but accommodation prices triple, so book months ahead.

Outside Festival season, Edinburgh is calmer but no less interesting. Stockbridge has the best independent shops and a Saturday market. Leith, the old port area, has become a proper food destination — Tom Kitchin's restaurant is there, along with loads of smaller places doing excellent seafood. The city feels manageable — you can walk from one end to the other in 40 minutes, but every walk reveals something you missed the last time.

## Things to See

The National Museum of Scotland on Chambers Street is free and genuinely brilliant — the top floor terrace has one of the best views of the Castle, and the galleries covering Scottish history and science are engaging without being dry. The Scottish National Gallery on the Mound has a strong permanent collection (Raeburn, Ramsay, and a surprising number of Impressionists) and the building itself is handsome.

For something different, the Real Mary King's Close takes you underground into 17th-century streets that were literally built over — it's atmospheric and the guided tours are excellent. Calton Hill at sunset is Edinburgh's answer to a viewpoint — Athenian columns, the Nelson Monument, and that classic panorama across the skyline. The Royal Botanic Garden is free, peaceful, and the glasshouses are worth the small entry fee, especially on a rainy day (and there will be rainy days — this is Scotland).

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Edinburgh is about as safe as it gets. The UK's allergen legislation (Natasha's Law) requires pre-packed food to carry full ingredient labelling, and restaurants must provide allergen information for every dish. In practice, Edinburgh restaurants handle this well — most menus flag allergens, and staff are trained to check with the kitchen. You can eat confidently at most restaurants. The main areas to check, as always, are desserts (some cakes and pastries use almonds or hazelnuts) and Asian restaurants (Thai and Chinese dishes may contain peanuts or cashews). But you'll be told clearly, and cross-contamination protocols are generally good.

For vegetarians, Edinburgh is outstanding. David Bann on St Mary's Street has been doing inventive vegetarian cooking since 2002 and remains excellent. Henderson's, first opened in 1962, claims to be the oldest vegetarian restaurant in the UK — the salad bar and deli downstairs are great for a quick lunch. Beyond the dedicated spots, Edinburgh's broader restaurant scene is very veggie-friendly: Baba on George Street does excellent Middle Eastern vegetarian mezze, and Harmonium on East London Street is a solid neighbourhood veggie café. Even the pubs have caught up — you'll find halloumi burgers, beetroot risotto, and proper vegetarian pies on most menus. The Stockbridge Market on Sundays has excellent street food including several vegetarian stalls.
