## Things to Do

Start with the Real Alcázar — book tickets online in advance because the queue is brutal, especially in spring. The palace complex is genuinely extraordinary: layer upon layer of Moorish, Mudéjar, and Renaissance architecture built over a thousand years. The gardens alone are worth an hour, all clipped hedges, tiled fountains, and peacocks wandering about. If it looks familiar, they filmed Dorne scenes from Game of Thrones here.

Walk through the Santa Cruz quarter afterwards. This is the old Jewish quarter, a maze of impossibly narrow lanes with whitewashed walls, wrought-iron balconies, and the occasional courtyard glimpsed through an open doorway. It's touristy in parts, but duck down any side street and you'll lose the crowds quickly. Plaza de Doña Elvira is a good spot to sit with a drink.

In the evening, head to Triana across the river for tapas. Calle Betis runs along the riverbank and is lined with bars, but the real action is in the streets behind — Calle San Jacinto and the streets off it. La Primera del Puente and Casa Cuesta are reliable, unpretentious choices. The tradition here is to order one or two dishes, finish your drink, then move to the next place. Nobody stays at one bar all night.

## The Vibe

Seville runs at its own speed. The city essentially shuts down between 2pm and 5pm — shops close, streets empty, and everyone disappears for lunch and a rest. Then it comes back to life around 6pm and stays up late. Dinner before 9pm marks you as a tourist; locals eat at 10pm or later. This takes some adjusting, but once you lean into the rhythm it's genuinely relaxing.

The city is smaller and more walkable than Barcelona or Madrid, and feels distinctly southern Spanish — there's a North African influence in the architecture, the orange trees lining every street, the jasmine scent in the evening air. Flamenco is a real, living art form here, not just a tourist show. For an authentic performance, try one of the smaller peñas flamencas (flamenco clubs) rather than the big tablao venues. Casa de la Memoria in Santa Cruz keeps things intimate and genuine.

One genuine warning: summer heat. July and August regularly hit 40-45°C. The city is designed for it (thick walls, shaded courtyards, late dining) but it's genuinely oppressive if you're not used to it. March to May is ideal — warm, sunny, the orange trees are in bloom. October and November are also lovely, with fewer tourists and comfortable temperatures.

## Things to See

Plaza de España is the showpiece — a vast semicircular building from the 1929 World's Fair, with tiled alcoves representing each Spanish province. It photographs incredibly well and it's free to visit. The moat around it has rowing boats for hire, which feels absurd but is actually quite fun.

The Cathedral is the largest Gothic cathedral in the world and contains Christopher Columbus's tomb (though there's debate about whether it's actually him). Climb the Giralda tower — the old minaret from the mosque that stood here before — for views across the city. The ramp inside was designed so the muezzin could ride a horse to the top.

Metropol Parasol (locals call it Las Setas — the mushrooms) is the enormous wooden lattice structure in Plaza de la Encarnación. Take the walkway across the top at sunset for panoramic views. The archaeological museum underneath, showing Roman and Moorish remains found during construction, is worth a look too.

For something quieter, walk through the Parque de María Luisa, the rambling park south of Plaza de España. The tiled benches, fountains, and shaded paths are perfect for late afternoon. The Museo de Artes y Costumbres Populares inside the park covers traditional Sevillano life — ceramics, textiles, flamenco history.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

Seville is one of the easier Spanish cities for nut allergies. The tapas tradition works in your favour — dishes are simple, ingredients are visible, and bartenders can tell you exactly what's in each plate because they're made fresh in small batches. The classics (jamón, cheese, olives, tortilla, fried fish, patatas bravas) contain no nuts at all.

The main risk areas are desserts and pastries. Turrón (almond nougat) is everywhere, especially around Christmas but available year-round. Pastelerías may use ground almonds in cakes and biscuits. Polvorones (crumbly shortbread) sometimes contain almonds. At restaurants, just mention your allergy when ordering dessert — "tengo alergia a los frutos secos" is the phrase to use, and staff in Seville are generally helpful about it.

Vegetarian tapas is where Seville genuinely shines. Espinacas con garbanzos — spinach simmered with chickpeas, cumin, and garlic — is a Seville original and you'll find it on almost every menu. Salmorejo (a thicker, creamier cousin of gazpacho, from nearby Córdoba) is superb. Berenjenas con miel is addictive — thick slices of aubergine, lightly battered, fried, and drizzled with dark cane honey. Pimientos asados (roasted peppers), alcachofas (artichokes, seasonal), and champiñones al ajillo (garlic mushrooms) round out a very satisfying meal.

The Alameda de Hércules neighbourhood, north of the centre, has the most progressive food scene — several restaurants here offer entirely vegetarian or vegan menus. Bar Eslava on Calle Eslava does creative modern tapas with strong vegetarian options. Around Triana and the more traditional bars, you'll still eat well as a vegetarian, but the selection is smaller and you may end up ordering the same three or four dishes on rotation.
