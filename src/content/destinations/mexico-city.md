## Things to Do

Mexico City rewards you for getting lost in it. Start in Roma Norte — the tree-lined streets around Calle Álvaro Obregón are packed with independent coffee shops, bookshops, and mezcalerías. Grab breakfast at one of the pavement cafés on Calle Orizaba and watch the neighbourhood wake up. From there, walk south into Condesa along Avenida Amsterdam, a circular boulevard built on an old horse-racing track that's now a leafy promenade ringed by Art Deco buildings.

For the best street food experience, head to the Mercado de Coyoacán — skip the tourist stalls around the main plaza and find the covered market where locals queue for tostadas de tinga and blue corn quesadillas stuffed with flor de calabaza (squash blossom). The taco stands on Calle de López in the Centro Histórico are open until the early hours and serve some of the city's best al pastor from enormous rotating spits.

Chapultepec Park is genuinely enormous — bigger than Hyde Park and Central Park combined. The Castillo de Chapultepec sits on a hill at its centre with views across the entire valley. Below it, the Museo Nacional de Antropología is one of the great museums of the world, housing the Aztec Sun Stone and entire reconstructed temple facades.

## The Vibe

Mexico City operates on its own frequency. It's simultaneously ancient and hypermodern, with pre-Columbian ruins sitting under colonial churches next to brutalist apartment blocks. The traffic is apocalyptic but somehow the city works — 22 million people moving through it with a patience and warmth that catches first-time visitors off guard.

Roma Norte and Condesa feel almost European — all pavement dining, natural wine bars, and design studios. Cross into the Centro Histórico and you're in a different world: vendors selling herbs and amulets outside the Catedral Metropolitana, organ grinders on every corner, and the Zócalo — one of the largest public squares on earth — opening up in front of you. Head south to Coyoacán for cobblestone streets and the Frida Kahlo Museum (book well in advance — it sells out weeks ahead), or east to Xochimilco for the famous trajineras, colourful punted boats on the ancient Aztec canal network.

## Things to See

The Palacio de Bellas Artes is the city's most photographed building — a marble Art Nouveau and Art Deco palace that hosts murals by Diego Rivera, David Alfaro Siqueiros, and Rufino Tamayo. Inside, the theatre has a glass curtain designed by Tiffany & Co depicting the Valley of Mexico's volcanoes. Time your visit for a Sunday morning when entry is free.

The Templo Mayor — the excavated main temple of the Aztec capital Tenochtitlán — sits right next to the cathedral in the Zócalo. It was only rediscovered in 1978 by electricity workers and the on-site museum does an excellent job of explaining just how extraordinary this city's layered history is.

Further afield, Teotihuacán is about an hour northeast. The Pyramid of the Sun and Pyramid of the Moon are staggering in scale — arrive at opening time (9am) before the tour buses and climb the Sun pyramid while it's still relatively cool. The Avenue of the Dead stretching between them gives a sense of just how large this pre-Aztec city was.

## Allergen & Dietary Detail

For nut allergies, Mexico City is generally manageable. The vast majority of Mexican street food and traditional cooking doesn't use tree nuts. The key watch-out is mole — there are dozens of varieties, and some (particularly mole negro from Oaxaca and mole almendrado) contain ground peanuts or almonds. Always ask "¿tiene cacahuates o nueces?" (does it have peanuts or nuts?) before ordering any mole dish. Pipián sauce, made from pumpkin seeds, is a safe and delicious alternative. In Roma and Condesa, restaurant staff typically speak English and understand allergy requests well.

Vegetarians will find the situation mixed. Traditional taquerías and fondas are built around meat, and even "vegetable" dishes might be cooked in lard. Your best bet for traditional food is to seek out quesadillas de huitlacoche (corn truffle — it's extraordinary), rajas con crema (roasted poblano strips in cream), esquites (corn in a cup with lime and chilli), and chapulines (toasted grasshoppers — technically not vegetarian but worth knowing about). Roma Norte has a strong modern food scene with places like Por Siempre Vegana serving plant-based tacos, and many of the newer restaurants on Calle Colima and Calle Tonalá offer dedicated vegetarian sections. The city is adapting fast, but outside the trendy neighbourhoods you'll need to be proactive about asking.
